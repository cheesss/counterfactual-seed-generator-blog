 Report · 2026-06-21 Repair & service capacity

The Subsea Cable Bottleneck Is Who Can Repair the Break

Submarine cables carry over 99% of international data; the risk is repair. The scarce asset is a ready cable ship, not more fiber. Two-thirds of repair ships reach end-of-life by 2040. Sustaining the fleet needs about $3 billion for roughly 20 ships.

 Report · 2026-06-21 Qualified materials & supply

The Shell Bottleneck Starts Before the Shell

Western rearmament looks like a shell-body problem; the gate is chemistry. A complete 155mm shot needs fill, propellant, primer, and fuze. The US had to rebuild domestic TNT production after decades of none.

 Report · 2026-06-21 Validation & certification evidence

The Grid Bottleneck Is Whether the Model Survives the Fault

A solar plant can pass every milestone and still fail at a fault. The new gate is proof the plant’s model matches real behavior. Disturbances since 2016 keep finding model-versus-reality mismatches. FERC Order 901 turned that gap into enforceable reliability standards.

 Report · 2026-06-21 Queue & slot scarcity

The AI Power Trade Is Becoming a Turbine Slot Trade

The standard story about AI power is easy to summarize: data centers are growing faster than the grid. That is true, but it is already becoming too broad to be useful. A hyperscaler can sign a power purchase agreement. A utility can announce new generation.

 Report · 2026-06-21 Permits & approvals

The AI Buildout’s Next Constraint Is the Air Permit

AI campuses need power faster than the grid can deliver it. The workaround is on-site combustion — gated by an air permit. One behind-the-meter plant can emit roughly 190 tons of NOx a year. xAI’s Memphis turbines became a Clean Air Act fight, not an IT story.

About

What Bottleneck Briefs is

Bottleneck Briefs traces a single industrial bottleneck to its irreducible constraint — the permit, qualified material, queue slot, certification, repair berth, or waste outlet that actually paces capacity — then names the cases and maps who controls it. Each column is built from a research appendix that logs every figure with its source, its date, and a confidence flag: two independent sources, or one primary. The site generator and editorial standard are open source, so the method can be read and reproduced.

View the source on GitHub →

How we source

Every load-bearing number is traced to a primary or near-primary document. High-confidence claims are stated plainly; medium-confidence claims are attributed; single-source or contested figures are flagged, never presented as settled fact. When sources conflict, the range is shown rather than a cherry-picked value.
