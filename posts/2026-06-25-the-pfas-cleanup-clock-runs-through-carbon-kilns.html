The public PFAS drinking-water story is usually told as a filter story. Water systems detect PFOA or PFOS, install treatment, and move the cost through rates, grants, state revolving funds, or settlements. That version is true as far as it goes, but it leaves out the part that repeats after the ribbon cutting. Once a treatment vessel works, PFAS mass has not disappeared. It has moved into granular activated carbon, ion exchange resin, membrane concentrate, sludge, or another residual stream.

That second stage is where the more durable constraint may sit. EPA's 2024 drinking-water rule set enforceable limits of 4.0 parts per trillion for PFOA and PFOS, with monitoring due by April 2027 and compliance originally due by April 2029. EPA's May 2026 proposal would keep those two standards while allowing eligible systems to seek more time, potentially to 2031, and would rescind four other federal PFAS standards. The calendar is politically and legally unsettled, but the physical work is already visible: pilot tests, engineering packages, vessel procurements, resin orders, permit amendments, media changeout plans, and disposal decisions.

The important distinction is between treatment as an installation and treatment as a service loop. Granular activated carbon can adsorb PFAS until breakthrough. Ion exchange can be highly selective. Reverse osmosis and nanofiltration can move PFAS out of product water. None of those pathways eliminates the need to decide what happens to the captured material. EPA's technical and disposal guidance describes GAC replacement with virgin or reactivated carbon, thermal reactivation in furnaces or kilns, PFAS-selective ion exchange resin that is generally single-use, and brine or concentrate streams from membrane systems. EPA also notes uncertainty around PFAS destruction during incineration and the need for more performance evidence in thermal treatment.

That makes the bottleneck one layer deeper than the equipment purchase. A utility can buy vessels. It can hire a water engineer. It can choose carbon, resin, or membranes. After breakthrough, the question changes: where can the spent material go, under which permit, on what transport schedule, with what emissions proof, and under whose liability standard?

Santa Clarita Valley Water's S Wells project is a clean example. The project is a $21.1 million ion exchange facility designed to restore wells S6, S7, and S8, with 4,000 gallons per minute of capacity and roughly 6,456 acre-feet per year of production. Vendor case materials around Santa Clarita show PFAS-selective resin and vessel systems operating at thousands of gallons per minute. The treatment decision looks like a water-system procurement. The recurring question is what happens to the spent resin when the resin has done its job.

Orange County Water District records show the same pattern in a different way. Committee materials for the City of Orange Wells 20, 21, and 22 project show a total project budget of about $13.56 million, including engineering, permitting, a construction contract, Aqueous Vets vessels, and Evoqua ion exchange resin. Those line items matter because they split the visible treatment plant from the media and residual-management chain. OCWD materials also refer to pre-purchase activity across up to 150 PFAS treatment vessels, while later Garden Grove procurement materials show additional PFAS treatment plants moving through a 2026 bid process.

Bemidji, Minnesota, shows why this does not end at startup. Barr describes an expanded drinking-water treatment plant built around GAC, with final capacity of 2,500 gallons per minute or 3.6 million gallons per day. A project-cost review describes GAC changeout timing moving from roughly two years after startup to roughly one year later in subsequent operation. The exact cadence depends on influent chemistry and design choices, but the business point is simple: recurring carbon handling can become a service rhythm, not a one-time capital event.

The clearest commercial signal is not a water utility at all. It is carbon reactivation capacity. Calgon Carbon announced an approximately $100 million expansion at its Columbus, Ohio plant to add 27 million pounds per year of drinking-water carbon reactivation capacity, with startup targeted for the first quarter of 2028. Kuraray described the same addition as about 12,300 metric tons per year. Calgon also said it operates six U.S. reactivation plants and two potable-water carbon reactivation kilns. That is not proof of a national shortage by itself. It is proof that specialized residual infrastructure is worth expanding.

The cost stack is bigger than vessels

EPA's economic analysis puts mean annualized rule costs near $1.55 billion, with public water system treatment costs accounting for most of the estimate. Federal funding is also large: $4 billion in DWSRF emerging-contaminants funding focused on PFAS and $5 billion for emerging contaminants in small or disadvantaged communities. Settlement money is another visible pool, including the public-water-supplier settlement announced by 3M. Those headline figures can make the problem look like a funding gap alone. The operating bottleneck is narrower: funding must turn into engineering capacity, vessels, media, lab capacity, state approvals, and residual outlets.

This is why disposal and destruction guidance matters. EPA identified a limited set of hazardous-waste combustion facilities and commercial carbon reactivation furnaces in its disposal guidance, while also discussing municipal waste combustors and sewage-sludge incinerators. It described thermal oxidizers above 1,100 degrees Celsius as potentially relevant for GAC reactivation, but also highlighted the need for more emissions characterization and site-specific evaluation. If regulators, states, utilities, or litigants demand stronger proof around PFAS destruction and air emissions, the bottleneck can tighten even when treatment vessels are available.

Ion exchange does not remove that problem. It changes its shape. PFAS-selective resin can be efficient in the vessel, but EPA's treatment support document describes conventional regeneration as generally ineffective for these resins and spent resin as typically going to incineration. Reverse osmosis and nanofiltration can produce very clean permeate, but they create concentrate streams. A project can therefore be technically successful at the tap while still creating a harder residual-management problem outside the fence line.

Who is exposed to the loop

The operating map crosses several groups. Carbon and reactivation capacity points toward Calgon/Kuraray and other activated-carbon suppliers. Resin and vessel choices point toward Xylem/Evoqua, Purolite/DuPont, LANXESS, Aqueous Vets, and similar equipment/media vendors. Engineering packages point toward water consultants such as Jacobs, Carollo, Kennedy Jenks, Barr, Brown and Caldwell, Hazen, AECOM, and Tetra Tech. Residual handling points toward carbon reactivators, hazardous-waste combustors, landfill operators, waste-service firms, transport providers, stack-testing firms, and PFAS laboratories. None of that is a buy list. It is a map of where the workload moves if the recurring residual stage becomes the pacing item.

The strongest version of the thesis would show public evidence of queueing: booked kiln slots, constrained reactivation contracts, explicit residual-management premiums, state permit conditions that slow media changeout, or utility board materials describing disposal risk as a schedule constraint. The weaker version is that vessel procurement, media supply, engineering labor, or political timing remain the main constraints. The current record supports attention to the residual loop, not certainty about a single national choke point.

What to watch

  • New potable-water carbon reactivation kiln announcements, permits, and startup dates.
  • Utility board packets that separate media replacement, hauling, disposal, reactivation, and liability.
  • State permit amendments that add residual-management or emissions-verification conditions.
  • Evidence that spent IX resin disposal becomes more expensive or harder to schedule.
  • Lab and stack-testing capacity tied to PFAS destruction verification.
  • Projects shifting from GAC to IX or membranes because residual pathways price differently.
  • Changes in federal or state rules that treat PFAS residuals as hazardous waste.

The PFAS cleanup clock may still be set by regulatory deadlines, but the recurring industrial clock is different. It starts after treatment works. Every breakthrough event asks the same operational question again: where does the captured PFAS go next?
