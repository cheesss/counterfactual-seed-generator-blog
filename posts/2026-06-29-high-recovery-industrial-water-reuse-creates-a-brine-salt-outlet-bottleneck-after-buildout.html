High-Recovery Industrial Water Reuse Creates A Brine/Salt Outlet Bottleneck After Buildout

Industrial water reuse has a hidden second act. The first act is recovery: factories, fabs, mines, data centers, and inland cities use RO, high-recovery RO, MLD, or ZLD to stretch scarce water supplies and reduce wastewater discharge.

By Bottleneck Briefs · Published 2026-06-29 · 5 min read

In brief

Industrial water reuse has a hidden second act. The first act is recovery: factories, fabs, mines, data centers, and inland cities use RO, high-recovery RO, MLD, or ZLD to stretch scarce water supplies and reduce wastewater discharge. The second act is salt: once the water is recovered, the remaining stream is smaller, denser, more corrosive, harder to treat, and harder to dispose of.

That second act is becoming a bottleneck in several high-growth industrial settings.

The pattern is not that reuse projects routinely fail. The pattern is subtler. Many projects work only because a parallel brine outlet is built: a brine reduction facility, a brine line, an evaporation pond, a thermal concentrator, a crystallizer, a deep injection well, a landfill path, or a dedicated beneficial-use strategy for salts. When that outlet is missing, late, expensive, or politically contested, the water-reuse project becomes constrained after it is already physically built.

Semiconductor fabs offer one of the clearest examples. Intel's Ocotillo campus in Chandler, Arizona operates in a region where treated wastewater is reused, not simply discharged away. Public accounts say high-TDS RO reject could not simply be sent to the POTW or into irrigation reuse because it would cycle up salts and hardness. Intel and Chandler therefore built the Ocotillo Brine Reduction Facility to lower TDS, recover water, and protect downstream reuse. Source: https://www.ultrafacilityportal.io/insights/end-user-insight%3A-water-strategy-at-intel%27s-ocotillo-site

Data centers show the same physics under a different load profile. In Quincy, Washington, Microsoft and the city built a $31 million reuse utility that recycles data center cooling water and offsets about 138 million gallons per year of demand. But the system still produces a small, highly concentrated brine stream. EPA's case study identifies a brine pond where salty brine from RO is managed by evaporation, and notes that the project required separate treatment to meet groundwater permit TDS limits. Source: https://www.epa.gov/waterreuse/water-reuse-case-study-quincy-washington

Municipal-industrial reuse makes the infrastructure dependency explicit. Southern California's Inland Empire Brine Line exists because inland water reuse and groundwater desalting need somewhere to put salt. SAWPA describes a 73-mile brine line carrying about 14 MGD against 30 MGD of capacity, removing about 500,000 pounds per day of salt, and supporting recycled water projects, groundwater desalters, and high-salinity industrial dischargers. Source: https://www.multi-statesalinitycoalition.com/wp-content/uploads/Concentrate-Mosher.pdf

Mining and coal seam gas show the endpoint problem most starkly. In Queensland, most CSG water can be treated for beneficial use, but residual brine and crystallized salt still need long-term management. Public sources describe brine storage ponds, crystallizers, mixed waste salt, and contested landfill-type salt strategies. Sources: https://environment.des.qld.gov.au/__data/assets/pdf_file/0018/240318/independent-review-brine-salt-management-report.pdf and https://www.shell.com.au/about-us/projects-and-locations/qgc/environment/water-management/reports/_jcr_content/root/main/section/list/list_item_copy_1895558752.multi.stream/1699250623403/dbe231bde6e6dc3d816bced65017e71163ad643f/15-brine-management-and-strategy.pdf

The underlying reason is simple: water recovery changes mass balance, not salt mass. A plant can recover 90%, 95%, or 98% of water, but most dissolved salts remain. As recovery rises, the last brine stream becomes more concentrated, more prone to scaling, and more likely to require thermal treatment or a regulated disposal endpoint. A recent brine-management review states that ZLD maximizes water recovery but is expensive and energy intensive; MLD can be more practical when brine disposal is available; and mechanical vapor compression and crystallizers remain common but energy-intensive final steps. Source: https://par.nsf.gov/servlets/purl/10614980

This does not mean high-recovery reuse is uneconomic or impractical. The disconfirming evidence is real. Where a brine line exists, where deep-well geology is suitable, where an ocean discharge is legal, where a pond is acceptable, or where a crystallizer and landfill path are already permitted, higher recovery can reduce liquid disposal volume and improve water resilience. Intel Ocotillo and Quincy are operational. The Inland Empire Brine Line is shared infrastructure. MLD can be rational when a residual brine outlet remains available.

The risk is architectural. Industrial water reuse is often planned as a water-supply project, but it behaves like a water-and-salt system. The projects most likely to hit bottlenecks are inland, high-growth, high-recovery projects with no low-cost liquid outlet, strict TDS or chloride limits, weak sewer acceptance, no regional brine line, limited pond land, difficult deep-well permitting, uncertain landfill acceptance, or a beneficial-use story that depends on an undeveloped salt market.

The practical test is not "Can the plant recover water?" It is "Where does every pound of salt go after recovery, under the actual permit, during normal operation and upset conditions?"

Key figures

  • Intel Ocotillo onsite water reclamation facility: public material says it can treat up to 9.1 million gallons per day. Source: https://download.intel.com/newsroom/archive/2025/en-us-2023-07-11-intels-ocotillo-campus-honored-for-water-stewardship.pdf
  • Chandler Ocotillo Brine Reduction Facility: public material says daily capacity is up to 2.8 million gallons per day of Intel manufacturing water; an ADEQ draft permit summary lists 3.0 MGD maximum average monthly flow. Sources: https://download.intel.com/newsroom/archive/2025/en-us-2023-07-11-intels-ocotillo-campus-honored-for-water-stewardship.pdf and https://static.azdeq.gov/pn/%E2%80%A2%20Old%20-%20Check%20then%20remove/app_chandlerobrf_es.pdf
  • Quincy Water Reuse Utility: EPA says operational since 2021, $31 million cost, about 138 million gallons per year of demand offset, and 10 years to plan, design, construct, and test. Source: https://www.epa.gov/waterreuse/water-reuse-case-study-quincy-washington
  • Inland Empire Brine Line: SAWPA presentation describes about 73 miles of brine line, about 14 MGD flow, 30 MGD capacity, roughly 6,000 mg/L TDS, and about 500,000 pounds per day of salt removed. Source: https://www.multi-statesalinitycoalition.com/wp-content/uploads/Concentrate-Mosher.pdf
  • Thermal brine concentration energy: recent review gives typical mechanical vapor compression energy intensity of about 20 to 40 kWh/m3 of brine and thermal crystallizer energy intensity of about 50 to 60 kWh/m3. Source: https://par.nsf.gov/servlets/purl/10614980
  • Coal mine ZLD: Debiensko equipment cost was reported at about $60 million, with evaporation operations beginning in 1993 and RO pretreatment completed in 1995; high energy consumption was identified as the main drawback. Source: https://www.reminewater.eu/portfolio/07-zero-liquid-discharge-application-in-coal-mining-a-case-study/
  • Queensland CSG: local reporting on the official action plan says more than 90% of CSG water is treated and made available for beneficial uses, while brine is stored in dedicated storage ponds pending long-term disposal strategy. Source: https://cqld.org.au/news/csg-brine-management/
  • Power cooling tower ZLD: an OSTI-hosted study found high-recovery RO could roughly double the levelized cost of water, while energy penalties were relatively small as a share of generation. Source: https://www.osti.gov/biblio/1766275
