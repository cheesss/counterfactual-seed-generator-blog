The Hospital Capacity Bottleneck Hiding in a Bag of Salt Water

By Bottleneck Briefs · Published 2026-06-22 · 9 min read

Hospital capacity is usually described in visible terms: beds, nurses, operating rooms, ventilators, and reimbursement. Those are real constraints. But the 2024 IV-fluid shortage showed that a hospital can also be throttled by something far less dramatic: a validated bag of sterile salt water.

That sounds too mundane to matter. Normal saline is cheap, bulky, familiar, and everywhere. It is not a breakthrough drug or a scarce biologic. It does not have the glamour of oncology, AI diagnostics, robotic surgery, or hospital-at-home software. Yet it sits underneath almost everything hospitals do: surgery, anesthesia, emergency care, dialysis, drug dilution, hydration, labor and delivery, infusion clinics, and basic inpatient care. When IV fluid becomes scarce, capacity does not disappear all at once. It becomes rationed through conservation protocols, allocation percentages, delayed procedures, changed order sets, and pharmacy workarounds.

Hurricane Helene made that hidden dependency visible. Flooding shut Baxter's North Cove, North Carolina facility, and the CDC described the site as the largest U.S. manufacturer of peritoneal dialysis and IV solutions. Reuters and the American Hospital Association later put the concentration more plainly: North Cove accounted for roughly 60% of U.S. IV fluids and peritoneal dialysis solutions, or about 1.5 million bags a day.

The important point is not that saline is hard chemistry. It is not. The bottleneck is the industrial system that turns sterile water, salt, plastic film, ports, tubing interfaces, form-fill-seal lines, sterilization, leak testing, particulate control, quality release, expiration dating, hospital contracts, and distribution into a product clinicians trust enough to put directly into patients. That system is efficient in normal times because it is concentrated, high-volume, and tightly validated. The same features make it brittle when a single site goes offline.

The first place the shortage appeared was allocation. In October 2024, Reuters reported that Baxter raised allocations of high-demand IV fluids to 60% of normal from 40% for direct customers, and to 60% from 10% for distributors. That was an improvement, but the language itself is the tell. The market was not clearing through price, nor was supply instantly replaced by competitors. Hospitals were being told how much of a basic input they could receive.

Premier's provider survey captured how quickly that moved from factory outage to clinical operations. In an October 7-8 survey of 257 provider respondents, more than 86% reported IV-fluid shortages, 88% said they were receiving less than half their requested orders, and 54% had 10 days or less of inventory compared with a typical 15 to 22 days. Seventeen percent had already canceled elective surgeries or other procedures; another 58% were considering cancellations if the situation did not improve. This is what a hidden industrial bottleneck looks like inside a hospital: not a headline collapse, but a quiet shrinking of optionality.

The federal response also shows what kind of bottleneck this was. The CDC told providers to assess inventory, conserve fluids, use oral alternatives where appropriate, consider other IV options, and review standing orders. FDA allowed temporary importation of sodium chloride bags from Baxter's Shanghai facility, even though the letter made clear that those specific products were not FDA-approved in the ordinary U.S. distribution channel. Virginia's health department noted FDA authorization for Baxter to extend use dates on more than 50 IV and irrigation product codes by up to 12 months.

Those are not normal procurement moves. They are emergency measures for a supply chain where the missing object is regulated, heavy, sterile, and hard to substitute quickly. A hospital can conserve, compound in limited cases, import under authorization, extend expiration dates, change clinical protocols, and shift to alternative products. But none of those actions is the same as creating a new validated large-volume parenteral plant.

The shortage then moved upstream and sideways. In December 2024, FDA's device center added empty IV containers, product code KPE, to the Medical Device Shortages List, with the shortage expected through March 2025. ASHP's fluid-shortage guidance warned that compounding sodium chloride from sterile water and concentrated sodium chloride is labor-intensive and can worsen the concentrated sodium chloride shortage. This is the pattern to watch in many industrial systems: when the obvious finished product is short, the true constraint often migrates to containers, components, quality systems, labor, documentation, or compatibility.

Baxter's recovery timeline is useful because it shows why redundancy cannot be summoned in a weekend. Medical Design & Outsourcing reported that a normal day at North Cove involved about 90 truckloads leaving the facility. Within three weeks after the storm, Baxter moved more than 450 truckloads of product from the site. With FDA permission, it ramped production at facilities in Canada, China, Ireland, and the U.K. A high-speed line representing roughly one-quarter of pre-storm production restarted by the end of October; a second line brought the site to about 50% of pre-storm production in mid-November; eight of ten lines were running by year-end; the last two restarted in January; and the plant returned to pre-hurricane production in February 2025. The company estimated the recovery required more than 2.5 million labor hours.

Alternative suppliers mattered, but they did not make the concentration irrelevant. B. Braun said it moved 60 truckloads, or 70,000 cases, of IV solutions north of Florida before Hurricane Milton and was increasing production at Irvine, California and Daytona Beach, Florida by 20%. It also said it would boost annual IV set output from Allentown, Pennsylvania by more than 30 million sets. Reuters reported that Fresenius Kabi had recently started IV-solution manufacturing at Wilson, North Carolina and was exploring ways to accelerate additional lines. That is meaningful relief. It is not the same as instantly replacing 1.5 million bags a day.

There is also history here. A 2018 New England Journal of Medicine perspective on saline shortages after Hurricane Maria made many of the same points. Saline was already in shortage before the hurricane. Baxter's Puerto Rico plant made small-volume saline bags. The market had only a few suppliers, and the others could not quickly replace the deficit. The U.S. needed more than 40 million bags of saline a month, and imports were costly because saline is heavy and bulky. The details differ, but the structure repeats: cheap generic sterile products can have expensive, concentrated, slow-to-replace manufacturing systems.

The strongest counterargument is that the acute crisis did end. FDA announced in August 2025 that the national shortage of 0.9% sodium chloride IV solutions was resolved. Baxter restored North Cove production. B. Braun and Fresenius added relief capacity. Australia also moved through its own IV-fluid shortage response, with the Therapeutic Goods Administration saying updated 2026 modelling showed stable supply across presentations.

That matters. The lesson is not that hospitals are permanently out of saline. The lesson is that sterile-fluid capacity behaves more like critical infrastructure than like an ordinary commodity. The bag is cheap; the validated system behind it is not. If the economics continue to favor concentrated production, thin inventories, single-supplier contracts, and minimal disaster redundancy, then the next disruption may not need to be very exotic. It could be a storm, a quality hold, a container shortage, a port disruption, a cyber outage, a labor shortage, or a problem with tubing and pump compatibility.

The practical indicators are straightforward. Watch FDA and ASHP shortage lists for saline, lactated Ringer's, dextrose, sterile water, irrigation fluids, empty IV containers, tubing, and peritoneal dialysis solutions. Watch supplier allocation percentages, hospital days-on-hand, procedure cancellations, emergency import authorizations, expiration-date extensions, and announced capex for U.S. large-volume parenteral lines. Watch whether hospitals move from single-supplier fluid contracts toward real dual sourcing without breaking pump and tubing compatibility.

The strategic asset is not salt water. It is resilient, geographically redundant, regulator-approved sterile-fluid capacity. A hospital system can look well supplied in beds and staff and still find itself constrained by a bag that normally disappears into the background. That is exactly why it is worth paying attention to.
