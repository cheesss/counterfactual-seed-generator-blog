The PFAS Cleanup Clock May Run Through Carbon Kilns, Not Filter Vessels

The public version of the PFAS drinking-water story is simple: thousands of water systems find “forever chemicals,” install filters, and pass the cost through rates, grants, settlements, or state revolving funds. The industrial version is less simple.

The public version of the PFAS drinking-water story is simple: thousands of water systems find “forever chemicals,” install filters, and pass the cost through rates, grants, settlements, or state revolving funds. The industrial version is less simple. The first constraint may be treatment vessels. The recurring constraint may be what happens after those vessels work.

EPA’s 2024 National Primary Drinking Water Regulation set enforceable drinking-water limits of 4.0 parts per trillion for PFOA and PFOS, with initial monitoring due by April 2027 and compliance originally due by April 2029. In May 2026, EPA proposed keeping the PFOA/PFOS standards while allowing some systems to request two additional years, potentially moving final compliance for those systems to 2031. The same 2026 action proposed rescinding the federal standards for PFHxS, PFNA, HFPO-DA, and the hazard-index mixture requirement. That makes timing politically and legally uncertain, but it does not erase the physical work already moving through the system: monitoring, pilot testing, engineering design, vessel procurement, resin selection, state permit amendments, media replacement planning, and disposal decisions.

The bottleneck worth isolating is not simply “PFAS treatment.” Granular activated carbon, ion exchange resin, and high-pressure membranes are known technologies. EPA’s technical support documents describe GAC breakthrough, carbon replacement with virgin or reactivated carbon, and thermal reactivation in multiple-hearth furnaces or rotary kilns. They describe PFAS-selective ion exchange resins as generally single-use, with conventional regeneration not effective for the way these resins bind PFAS. They also describe spent resin as typically incinerated, while noting uncertainty around the fate of PFAS during incineration and the possibility that future air or hazardous-waste rules could change spent-resin management.

That means the recurring gate may sit one layer after the installation contract. A utility can buy vessels. It can choose GAC or IX. It can hire Jacobs, Carollo, Kennedy Jenks, Barr, Brown and Caldwell, Hazen, AECOM, or another water engineer. But after breakthrough, the PFAS mass is no longer in the well. It is in carbon, resin, brine, sludge, or a residual stream. The question becomes: where can that residual go, on what schedule, at what liability standard, with what emissions evidence, and under whose permit?

The named projects already show the pattern. Santa Clarita Valley Water’s S Wells PFAS treatment project is a $21.1 million ion exchange facility designed to restore Wells S6, S7, and S8, with 4,000 gallons per minute of capacity and roughly 6,456 acre-feet per year of production. Purolite and Evoqua/Xylem case materials describe Santa Clarita systems using PFAS-selective ion exchange resins and vessel systems capable of thousands of gallons per minute. A Journal AWWA review of U.S. PFAS treatment plants describes SCV Water’s Rio Vista facility as a 9 million gallon per day IX system using Evoqua, Purolite PSR2+, and PFA694E media, with spent media incinerated.

Orange County Water District shows the procurement machinery at larger scale. In 2022 committee materials, the District discussed City of Orange Wells 20, 21, and 22 PFAS treatment systems, CEQA categorical exemption work, State Division of Drinking Water permit amendments, and accelerated design using Jacobs treatment testing, Carollo planning, Kennedy Jenks design, Aqueous Vets vessels, and Evoqua IX resin. The same materials listed a City of Orange project budget of $13.56 million, including $1.34 million for Aqueous Vets vessels and $1.22 million for Evoqua resin, and referenced RFQs to pre-purchase up to 150 PFAS treatment vessels. In 2026, OCWD issued an RFB for Garden Grove Wells 22, 26, and 27 PFAS treatment plants, with mandatory pre-bid and bid dates already scheduled. Its draft budgets for FY2025-26 and FY2026-27 include tens of millions of dollars for producer PFAS treatment facilities.

Bemidji, Minnesota shows the GAC version. Public project descriptions and AWWA cost-review material describe a drinking-water plant expanded from 1,500 gallons per minute to 2,500 gallons per minute, with greensand filtration followed by Calgon Filtrasorb 400 GAC. The AWWA review says the first carbon changeout occurred roughly two years after Phase 1 startup and the second one about a year later. A Florida AWWA presentation on PFAS treatment evaluations describes offsite carbon reactivation and a 45- to 60-day cycle from removal to return, with reactivated carbon “topped off” by virgin media. That is not just a capital project. It is a service loop.

This is why Calgon Carbon’s February 2026 expansion matters. The company announced nearly $100 million of investment at its Columbus, Ohio plant, adding 27 million pounds per year of drinking-water carbon reactivation capacity, targeted for first-quarter 2028. Kuraray, Calgon’s parent, described the same project as 12,300 metric tons per year of added capacity and linked the investment to U.S. PFAS regulation. Calgon said Columbus is one of six U.S. reactivation plants and one of two with potable water carbon kilns. The date is important: first-quarter 2028 is close to the 2029 compliance clock and still relevant if some systems receive extensions into 2031.

The physical stack runs at least three layers upstream.

First layer: water systems must meet a parts-per-trillion rule using equipment sized in gallons per minute or million gallons per day. The system-level constraint is not merely chemical; it is hydraulic, spatial, and regulatory.

Second layer: the treatment decision changes the residual. GAC uses bituminous coal, lignite, peat, wood, or coconut-shell feedstocks and becomes PFAS-loaded carbon after breakthrough. IX can fit tighter sites and may run longer for some PFAS profiles, but PFAS-selective resin is usually single-use and becomes a disposal item. RO and NF can remove PFAS at high rates, but create concentrate streams and carry higher energy, reject-water, and brine-management burdens.

Third layer: residual management runs into thermal assets, emissions evidence, and liability. EPA’s 2024 interim destruction and disposal guidance lists reactivation, incineration, and landfill as management options for spent GAC; incineration or landfill for spent IX resin; and permitted discharge or other management for membrane brine. It also says there were no specific federal PFAS disposal requirements as of December 2023. At the same time, EPA notes that GAC reactivation units with thermal oxidizers above 1,100°C and scrubbers can potentially destroy PFAS, while emphasizing the need for more data. EPA also says it is not aware of complete full-scale emissions characterizations for several thermal unit categories and recommends performance testing before PFAS materials are accepted, including feed characterization, combustion conditions, parent destruction, products of incomplete combustion, and air sampling.

That is the investable translation: the residual chain may monetize differently from the vessel chain. The vessel project is episodic. The media changeout, reactivation, lab verification, transport, emissions testing, and disposal pathway are recurring. Utilities may spend once on the plant, but they spend repeatedly on the adsorbent loop.

The legal and funding backdrop reinforces the point without settling it. EPA’s economic analysis put mean annualized costs for the rule around $1.55 billion, with treatment costs making up the majority. The same document excludes the possibility that PFAS-contaminated wastes become hazardous wastes under RCRA from the central cost case, treating that as a sensitivity. It also notes comments raising limited lab and disposal capacity, supply-chain disruptions, and personnel shortages. Federal funding is meaningful: the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law provided $4 billion for Drinking Water State Revolving Fund emerging contaminants and $5 billion for the Emerging Contaminants in Small or Disadvantaged Communities program, with a PFAS focus. Settlements also matter. 3M finalized a large public-water-supplier settlement, and Reuters/AP reported in June 2026 that Chemours agreed to a $450 million federal settlement framework involving civil penalties, pollution controls, and drinking-water provisions. But grants and settlements solve the finance constraint more cleanly than they solve the residual-chain constraint.

The contradiction is real. If EPA finalizes a broad delay or narrower rule, the near-term scramble eases. If courts or future administrations weaken the rule, some projects may slow. If PFAS concentrations are lower than feared, GAC beds may last longer. If carbon reactivation proves clean, scalable, and accepted by state regulators, the bottleneck becomes less severe. If utilities choose IX because of site constraints, the bottleneck may migrate from potable carbon kilns to single-use resin supply and incineration or landfill liability. If RO/NF wins in some geographies, the problem shifts again into brine and concentrate management.

Still, the direction of travel is visible. Municipal PFAS treatment is moving from pilot studies into procurement packages, vessel pre-purchases, DDW permit amendments, bid walks, and plant startups. As these plants move from design to operation, the market’s attention should move from “who sells the filter?” to “who owns the permitted, certified, defensible path for the PFAS-laden residual after breakthrough?”

The answer may determine which part of the PFAS compliance chain captures durable economics: equipment, media, engineering services, laboratory testing, thermal treatment, regulated waste management, or some combination of all five.
