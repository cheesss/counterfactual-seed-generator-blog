Green Hydrogen Electrolyzer Feedwater Becomes An Ultrapure-Water O&M Bottleneck

Green hydrogen's water problem is often framed too simply. The public debate usually starts with liters of water per kilogram of hydrogen. That number matters, especially in arid regions.

By Bottleneck Briefs · Published 2026-06-29 · 5 min read

In brief

Green hydrogen's water problem is often framed too simply. The public debate usually starts with liters of water per kilogram of hydrogen. That number matters, especially in arid regions. But the operating problem emerging from project evidence is more specific: electrolyzers need water that has been purified, polished, monitored, and kept within tight chemistry limits.

The theoretical water input for electrolysis is about 9 liters per kilogram of hydrogen. Real-world estimates often rise to roughly 20-30 liters per kilogram once purification and cooling are counted [S4]. At plant scale, industry estimates put ultrapure-water demand around 163-200 liters per hour per MW, or about 200 m3/h for a 1 GW plant [S2]. Those figures can become controversial where water is scarce. Yet they still do not capture the full operational challenge.

PEM electrolyzers generally require the tightest water chemistry. Public sources commonly refer to sub-1 uS/cm water, and many PEM applications target around 0.1 uS/cm or lower [S3] [S16] [S29] [S30]. Alkaline electrolyzers can often accept higher conductivity, but they still require demineralized water and operate with caustic electrolyte loops that make water chemistry and impurity management a plant-level concern [S1] [S10] [S17] [S18]. AEM electrolyzers are the possible future relief valve, because they are being developed for lower-cost operation and broader feed tolerance. But today's commercial AEM documentation still specifies purified water, and the research literature does not yet support broad claims that low-grade water is commercially solved [S8] [S9] [S45].

This is why the feedwater system is moving from background utility to critical infrastructure. A hydrogen plant may need pretreatment, reverse osmosis, electrodeionization, mixed-bed polishing, degassing, final deionization, conductivity monitoring, silica and chloride control, resin replacement, and wastewater or brine management. Water-treatment vendors now market hydrogen-specific systems, and named projects show that those systems are not optional add-ons [S12] [S13] [S14] [S15] [S16].

The project trail is instructive. HySynergy in Denmark uses alkaline electrolysis and a dedicated water-treatment system supplying 4,500 liters per hour of ultrapure water below 5 uS/cm [S17] [S18]. The Kassoe e-methanol project uses a 50 MW PEM electrolyzer and a water system designed for 10,000 liters per hour below 0.1 uS/cm, using RO, degassing, EDI, and mixed-bed polishing [S29] [S30]. Holland Hydrogen 1, a 200 MW project in Rotterdam, has a dedicated ultrapure-water equipment supplier, while Shell says the project is under construction with commissioning expected in late 2026 [S27] [S28].

Water sourcing can make the bottleneck harsher. Plug Power's Mendota project was framed around recycled wastewater rather than groundwater, with a tertiary treatment plant intended to convert secondary treated sewage effluent into recycled industrial water for hydrogen production [S21] [S22]. In Texas, the Limestone project environmental assessment describes reclaimed wastewater, RO/DI treatment, and a 30-year supply agreement for at least 350,000 gallons per day [S24]. Floodlight's reporting shows why this matters politically: several green-hydrogen projects and hubs are being proposed in high or extreme water-stress areas, and local concern often centers on aquifers, wastewater reuse, and desalination residuals [S23].

The technical risk is not abstract. Impurities can degrade stacks. Cations can impair PEM membrane conductivity. Chloride can enable chlorine-evolution and corrosion pathways. Silica and boron can escape simple conductivity-based monitoring because they are weakly ionized or difficult to reject under some treatment conditions [S1] [S46] [S47] [S48]. This creates an O&M problem: the plant must not only buy water, but continuously prove that water is clean enough.

Direct seawater electrolysis is the main counterargument. It could, if commercialized, bypass part of the desalination and ultrapure-water chain. But current evidence does not yet displace the bottleneck thesis. Reviews and public research programs treat direct seawater and low-grade-water electrolysis as promising but unresolved. The Joint Research Centre concludes that no research or industrial project has yet demonstrated clear benefits of direct over indirect seawater electrolysis [S40]. AEM research has produced long-duration seawater demonstrations, but these do not yet prove commercial multi-year stack life across industrial operating conditions [S41] [S45].

The conclusion is not that green hydrogen is impossible or that water will dominate every project. Electricity cost, electrolyzer utilization, offtake, grid interconnection, hydrogen transport, storage, and stack capex can still be larger bottlenecks. The conclusion is narrower and more operational: feedwater is becoming a specialized ultrapure-water reliability system. In water-rich sites, the issue may be hidden inside O&M contracts. In water-stressed or wastewater-fed sites, it becomes visible in permits, treatment skids, residuals, and public acceptance. Either way, it is more than a simple water-volume input.
