Hospital Wastewater AMR Surveillance Becomes Infection-Control Workflow Infrastructure

Hospital wastewater used to sit outside the core infection-control conversation. It was a disposal route, not a dashboard. That boundary is now eroding. The reason is antimicrobial resistance.

By Bottleneck Briefs · Published 2026-06-29 · 6 min read

Hospital wastewater used to sit outside the core infection-control conversation. It was a disposal route, not a dashboard. That boundary is now eroding.

The reason is antimicrobial resistance. Hospitals concentrate patients, antibiotics, drains, sinks, toilets, devices, staff movement, and vulnerable immune systems. That makes them not just places where resistant organisms are treated, but places where resistant organisms can circulate, hide, and reappear. Wastewater surveillance is becoming a way to inspect that hidden circulation.

The important question is not whether laboratories can detect resistance genes in sewage. They can. The important question is whether a hospital can turn those detections into an infection-control workflow.

A laboratory can detect blaKPC, blaNDM, blaVIM, blaOXA-48-like, blaCTX-M, vanA, mecA, or other resistance markers. But a hospital has to answer harder questions. Where did the signal come from? Which ward or plumbing branch contributed it? Is the source an infected patient, a colonized patient, a long-lived drain biofilm, a contaminated sink, or a mixture of all of them? Does the signal match clinical cultures? Should infection-prevention teams screen patients, adjust cleaning, isolate a room, investigate sinks, replace fixtures, or wait for confirmation?

That is why the problem is moving from detection to workflow infrastructure.

The University Hospital Limerick case shows the shift. Researchers removed 20 pipe sections from sinks, showers, and toilets in a ward with repeated multidrug-resistant healthcare-associated infection outbreaks. They found high antimicrobial-resistance gene burden in wastewater-pipe biofilms and compared the pipe microbiology with five clinical isolates from patients. The study found high similarity between the built environment and patient isolates, and the university release stated that hospital management and engineering teams used the results to guide interventions and design thinking around sinks and showers. [S3][S4][S5] Full URLs: https://pure.ul.ie/en/publications/large-scale-characterization-of-hospital-wastewater-system-microb/ ; https://pureadmin.qub.ac.uk/ws/portalfiles/portal/527972067/1_s2.0_S0195670123002876_main.pdf ; https://www.ul.ie/ehs/medicine/news/unique-new-ul-led-research-finds-bacteria-resistant-to-antibiotics-in-hospital

That is not just a sequencing story. It is a facilities story. It involves sink location, drain design, splash risk, maintenance access, decontamination, ward refurbishment, infection-prevention policy, and clinical interpretation.

CDC's healthcare AMR materials point in the same direction. CDC notes that resistant germs can spread within and between healthcare facilities, that fecal waste can carry antibiotics, antifungals, and resistant germs into sinks and toilets, and that these organisms can survive in drains and splash back into patient-care environments. CDC also states that healthcare wastewater is especially challenging because inpatients may shed some of the deadliest resistant organisms. [S15] Full URL: https://www.cdc.gov/antimicrobial-resistance/causes/healthcare.html

This is where wastewater surveillance becomes operational. A useful system needs a plumbing map, a sampling plan, assays, clinical correlation, escalation rules, engineering access, and a closing loop. It has to connect the sewer signal to the infection-prevention committee and to the facilities team that can actually inspect, clean, redesign, or replace a reservoir.

The strongest public-health logic is early warning. Facility-level wastewater can potentially detect circulating or emerging resistant organisms before they are found through clinical cultures alone. Poretsky et al. tested that logic at a Chicago long-term acute care hospital, sampling twice weekly for six months and comparing passive, composite, and grab approaches along with qPCR and dPCR for C. auris and carbapenemase genes. The final article was not publicly accessible in full during this search session, but the accessible preprint and abstract indicate that the work was designed around operational method selection, not just discovery. [S7][S8] Full URLs: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/389930956_Sewer_monitoring_for_antimicrobial_resistance_genes_and_organisms_at_healthcare_facilities ; https://academic.oup.com/jid/article/233/1/38/8259926

The caution is equally important. WHO's 2025 summary says AMR wastewater and environmental surveillance has proof-of-concept support, but it also says routine operational AMR programs integrated into broader public-health action are not yet established. WHO highlights unresolved issues around sampling sites, laboratory methods, sensitivity, specificity, and health-impactful use cases. [S2] Full URL: https://cdn.who.int/media/docs/default-source/wash-documents/wash-related-diseases/wes-summary-for-amr---final-1-dec.pdf?download=true&amp;sfvrsn=fb4aadba_3

So the answer is yes, with uncertainty. Hospital wastewater AMR surveillance is becoming an infection-control workflow infrastructure problem. But the field is still in the translation phase. The bottleneck is not only whether a lab can find resistance. It is whether a hospital can decide what the signal means and act on it without overreacting, underreacting, or mistaking plumbing ecology for patient epidemiology.

In brief

  • the argument is directionally confirmed: hospital wastewater AMR surveillance is moving beyond laboratory detection toward infection-control workflow, facility engineering, and operational response.
  • The strongest evidence comes from hospital plumbing-reservoir studies, method-comparison pilots, CDC healthcare wastewater framing, WHO implementation guidance, and systematic evidence that hospital water systems can sustain resistant organisms.
  • The main bottleneck is actionability: translating a sewer signal into a ward-level or fixture-level intervention that infection-prevention and facilities teams can execute.
  • Clinical microbiology remains necessary. Wastewater does not reliably distinguish infection from colonization, persistent plumbing biofilm, or non-patient sources.
  • The field is not yet standardized. WHO states that established routine AMR wastewater programs integrated into broader public-health action are not yet demonstrated.
  • The highest-value research direction is paired wastewater, patient-screening, clinical-culture, antibiotic-use, occupancy, and plumbing-infrastructure data.

Key figures

  • 1.91 million deaths attributable to bacterial AMR and 8.22 million deaths associated with bacterial AMR are forecast annually by 2050 in a 2024 Lancet Global Research on Antimicrobial Resistance analysis. This is global burden context, not a hospital-wastewater-specific estimate. [S22][S23] Full URLs: https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(24)01867-1/fulltext ; https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39299261/
  • CDC states that seven urgent or serious antimicrobial-resistant bacterial threats can cause healthcare-associated infections and that treating infections caused by these bacteria costs more than USD 5.6 billion annually in the United States. [S15] Full URL: https://www.cdc.gov/antimicrobial-resistance/causes/healthcare.html
  • CDC reports more than 600,000 swabs collected through its AR Lab Network colonization-screening work and says more than 97% of US acute-care hospitals report antibiotic-stewardship programs. These are clinical-surveillance and stewardship figures, not wastewater figures. [S13] Full URL: https://www.cdc.gov/antimicrobial-resistance/programs/AR-investments.html
  • A 2017 Clinical Infectious Diseases review identified 32 reports over 20 years involving carbapenem-resistant organisms in healthcare water environments; drains, sinks, and faucets were frequent reservoirs; 72% of reports described outbreak control; and just over one-third reported elimination of the organism from the water environment. [S18] Full URL: https://academic.oup.com/cid/article/64/10/1435/2990266
  • The University Hospital Limerick study analyzed 20 pipe sections and five clinical isolates from a ward with repeated multidrug-resistant healthcare-associated infection outbreaks. Reported clinical-isolate ARG counts were 24, 77, 26, 60, and 54 across the five isolates. [S3][S4] Full URLs: https://pure.ul.ie/en/publications/large-scale-characterization-of-hospital-wastewater-system-microb/ ; https://pureadmin.qub.ac.uk/ws/portalfiles/portal/527972067/1_s2.0_S0195670123002876_main.pdf
  • Poretsky et al. sampled a Chicago long-term acute care hospital twice weekly for six months and evaluated C. auris plus five carbapenemase genes: blaKPC, blaNDM, blaVIM, blaIMP, and blaOXA-48-like. The accessible preprint states that all target genes were detected and that passive sampling processed immediately with centrifugation, bead-beating, and dPCR was most reliable in that study. [S8] Full URL: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/389930956_Sewer_monitoring_for_antimicrobial_resistance_genes_and_organisms_at_healthcare_facilities
  • A 2026 Journal of Infectious Diseases skilled-nursing-facility study collected weekly wastewater effluent samples from three Georgia skilled nursing facilities over six months, tested 73 enriched samples, recovered 209 isolates across more than 20 genera, and found 100% concordance between WGS and PCR confirmation for screened carbapenemase genes. The article is marked "Available for Purchase," but the abstract is publicly visible. [S26] Full URL: https://academic.oup.com/jid/article/233/1/59/8322851
