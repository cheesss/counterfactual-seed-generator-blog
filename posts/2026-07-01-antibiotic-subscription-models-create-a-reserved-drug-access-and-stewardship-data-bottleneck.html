Central exploratory claim: antibiotic subscription and “delinked” pull-incentive models may not primarily create a simple procurement bottleneck; they create a reserved-drug access, stewardship, and validation-evidence bottleneck. The payer is not only buying vials. The payer is buying credible access to a scarce drug while preserving low-volume use, so the.

Deepest nameable constraint: validated antimicrobial-use and antimicrobial-resistance data pipelines from hospital laboratory information systems, pharmacy/eMAR systems, EHRs, and stewardship workflows into payer-usable evidence systems. In the US, CDC NHSN AUR explicitly requires HL7 CDA electronic submission for AU/AR data and says manual entry is.

How the constraint could move

  • Claim: as the UK, Japan, Canada, and possible US/EU models scale, payers require increasingly granular resistance, use, stewardship, diagnostic, and supply evidence before renewing or assigning high-value subscription bands.
  • UK: £100m/year England budget in 2024/25 procurement; £5m-£20m/year value bands.
  • US PASTEUR proposal: $6bn model; $75m-$300m/year contracts if enacted.
  • CDC AUR: eligible hospitals/CAHs must submit AU and AR data or claim exclusion; 180 continuous reporting days.

What could break the thesis

  • The core bottleneck may be political, not data.
  • Evidence: PASTEUR has been repeatedly introduced but not enacted; no executed US subscription contracts were found.
  • Implication: in the US, the binding constraint is authorization and appropriation before data systems matter.

Research context

  • This is first-pass exploratory research, not Deep Research, not a final thesis, and not an investment judgment. No buy/sell recommendation is made.
  • 1. Reserved and last-resort antibiotics are intentionally used sparingly to slow resistance.
  • 2. Sparse use suppresses ordinary sales revenue, so a volume-based revenue model does not reliably support development, launch, or availability.
  • 3. Subscription models substitute fixed or semi-fixed access payments for sales-volume revenue.
  • 4. A fixed payment creates a payer verification problem: the payer must know whether the antibiotic is valuable, available, supplied reliably, used appropriately, monitored for resistance, and not promoted into overuse.
  • 5. That verification problem depends on hospital antimicrobial-use data, lab susceptibility data, pathogen/resistance data, prescribing indication, formulary controls, diagnostic turnaround, and supply-chain evidence.
  • 7. If the data constraint eases, the likely next bottlenecks are political appropriation, rapid diagnostics, domestic or secure manufacturing, environmental certification, and the underlying scientific pipeline.
  • Chosen constraint shape: validation-evidence.
  • Main uncertainty: subscription models may reveal multiple moving bottlenecks rather than one durable bottleneck. In early programs, supply guarantee and political procurement may bind first; as programs scale, validation evidence and stewardship data may bind next; if that is solved, scientific pipeline and diagnostic turnaround may dominate.
  • 1. The UK is the strongest operational example of a true value-assessed subscription model. NICE selected cefiderocol and ceftazidime-avibactam in the pilot, then NHS England expanded the model in 2024. The commercial model uses annual payment bands and fixed-fee contracts delinked from prescribing volume, but suppliers must satisfy evidence, supply.
  • 2. The UK procurement documents directly support the argument’s “stewardship data bottleneck” hypothesis. The fixed payment is not simply a grant; it is tied to specified performance, including supply, stewardship, and surveillance. The ITT evidence stage determines which annual subscription value a product receives.
  • 3. Sweden’s model is more availability-reserve than innovation pull. It guarantees minimum annual revenue in exchange for stock and rapid delivery, with five products from four companies in the 2020-2022 pilot. The evaluation says the model improved access, but it also surfaced stock sizing and waste because some unsold products had to be cancelled.
  • 4. Japan’s program makes the stewardship-data bottleneck unusually explicit. MHLW evaluates companies on sales-volume compliance, guideline adherence, appropriate-use activities, and AMR drug development. A 2025 review identified that non-CRE prescriptions were about three times CRE prescriptions, while only CRE-oriented reporting was initially central.
  • 5. The US PASTEUR Act remains primarily a political bottleneck because it has been repeatedly introduced without final enactment. However, the 2026 one-pager embeds exactly the evidence burden expected by the argument: subscription contracts would require reliable supply, public reporting of resistance data, stewardship and education, environmental best.
  • 6. CDC NHSN AUR is a concrete data-infrastructure bottleneck. CMS AUR reporting is required for eligible hospitals/CAHs, and CDC’s AUR protocol requires electronic submission using HL7 CDA specifications. Manual entry is unavailable for key AU/AR reporting, which makes vendor systems, LIS data, pharmacy/eMAR data, and validation a gating layer.
  • 7. WHO GLASS and ECDC EARS-Net show that surveillance capacity is global but uneven. WHO reports more than 23 million bacteriologically confirmed cases in the 2025 GLASS report, with 104 countries reporting 2023 data, but surveillance still depends on national systems, laboratories, WHONET/LIS workflows, and AST quality.
  • 8. The scientific pipeline remains a competing bottleneck. WHO says the antibacterial pipeline is inadequate despite a larger count of products: many candidates are not innovative, and few target priority pathogens with meaningful novelty.
  • Irreducible constraint reached: not a single molecule or payer budget alone, but the validated data-and-stewardship evidence stack needed to justify paying for access while suppressing volume. The practical “owner” is fragmented: hospitals own source data; vendors own systems; CDC/WHO/ECDC own standards and surveillance programs; payers own procurement.

The evidence base is source-heavy but still preliminary. Treat the visuals below as a map of where the constraint may sit, not as a ranking of companies or a finished forecast.
