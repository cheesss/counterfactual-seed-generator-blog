Source Diffusion 20260629 06 Green Hydrogen Electrolyzer Feedwater

Electrolytic hydrogen needs about 9–10 litres of ultrapure water per kilogram. A 1 GW electrolyzer draws ~200 m³/h — a midsize town's daily water. Feed-water quality is the single biggest maintenance variable in a stack.

By Bottleneck Briefs · Published 2026-06-29 · 8 min read

In brief

  • Electrolytic hydrogen needs about 9–10 litres of ultrapure water per kilogram.
  • A 1 GW electrolyzer draws ~200 m³/h — a midsize town's daily water.
  • Feed-water quality is the single biggest maintenance variable in a stack.
  • Large draws trigger abstraction permits; drought regions can gate projects.
  • Developers now finance whole treatment plants to lock in supply.

Green hydrogen developers often fixate on stack cost and renewable power price, but feedwater deserves equal attention. A green-hydrogen facility is effectively a high-pressure electrolysis plant plus a miniature ultra-pure water plant. For example, Everfuel’s HySynergy 1 project (20 MW, Fredericia refinery, Denmark) uses city tap water (no chlorine) that is softened and sent through a double-pass RO system (www.eurowater.com). The result is 4.5 m³/h of <5 µS/cm water into the alkaline electrolyzer (www.eurowater.com). That project’s main remaining issue at Q1 2024 was a mechanical fitting in the de-oxygenation system, not water quality, but the complexity of the water train is undeniable. Other large projects take no risks: in California’s Central Valley (Mendota), Plug Power is financing an entire tertiary wastewater treatment plant for the city to ensure a stable “purple pipe” water supply. When complete (~2025), Plug’s 300 MW facility will consume about 120,000 gal/day of recycled water to make 30 metric tons of H₂ per day (resources.plugpower.com). Plug emphasizes that securing this new water source was “fundamental” to launching the hydrogen plant (resources.plugpower.com). In short, project planners must treat water as infrastructure, not background utility.

Beyond these cases, industry engineering reports and consultancies highlight water as a critical factor. A 2025 German study (Drees & Sommer) for a hypothetical 1 GW electrolyzer notes ~3.57 million m³/year raw water needed vs. 200 m³/h electrolyzer throughput (www.dreso.com) (excluding cooling). Regulators in water-scarce regions prepare guidance: Natural Resources Wales explicitly classifies large electrolysis as needing an abstraction licence (>20 m³/d) and advises developers that “water availability… could be a limiting factor” (naturalresources.wales) (naturalresources.wales). In practice, this means any ambitious plant must schedule months or years to secure permits, or use alternative sources (wastewater, desalination) which add cost and risk.

Why it matters now: The push for green hydrogen is racing ahead, but markets and policy often underweight hydraulic constraints. Hydrogen visions from the EU’s 10 GW plan to local hydrogen valleys assume ample water. Yet the engineering details say otherwise. For instance, Riefilt (a specialized water-treatment vendor) calculates that a 1 GW plant needs 3,000–5,000 m³/day of raw water (ground, surface, or treated effluent) (riefilt.com). That is essentially the daily demand of a midsize town. Losing even a portion of that supply (e.g. due to drought rules) can derail production. If hydrogen hubs are placed in arid areas (North Africa or West Texas, or near agriculture), water conflict can overshadow the touted benefits.

Caution on resilience: Electrolyzers tolerate almost no impurities. A PEM electrolyzer requires deionized water with resistivity ideally >10 MΩ·cm (www.hovogen.com); alkaline systems are similarly finicky. Pollutants like calcium, magnesium, chloride or iron rapidly degrade membranes and catalysts (riefilt.com) (www.hovogen.com). In a maintenance handbook, experts warn that even brief exposure to hard water causes “measurable performance loss” that is not fully reversible (www.hovogen.com). Continuous polishing loops (mixed-bed or EDI recirculation) become mandatory. Major equipment OEMs (Ovivo’s H2PW™ units, Evoqua/FGD systems, etc.) now market integrated electrolyzer-feedwater systems, sometimes on a BOOT basis (www.ovivowater.com), reflecting that customers want water “with guarantees” and outsourced operations. A US hydrogen producer even signed a 10+ year BOO with Evoqua covering all maintenance and chemicals for the water system.

Who controls this bottleneck: It falls to water-network operators, treatment-vendor cos, and regulators. Companies like Veolia, Suez, Evoqua, Ovivo, Grundfos/Eurowater supply the ultrapurification train (membranes, EDI modules, pumps). They hold know-how and incremental pricing power: for specialized mixed-bed resins and service, only a few aggressive players act in this niche. On the regulatory side, agencies that allocate abstraction rights or set wastewater limits effectively gate hydrogen rollout. Financially, hydrogen offtake deals may need to include clauses for water supply contracts or permit milestones. (Plug’s project for example is financing the city’s water plant to lock in its feedwater.) Publicly traded exposure is limited but includes obvious names: hydrogen “pure-play” Plug Power (PLUG), electrolysis OEMs like Siemens Energy (ticker ENR) and Thyssenkrupp Nucera (TNeb.DE) whose orders depend on water system readiness, and specialized water-treatment groups (e.g. Ecolab (ECL) or Pentair (PNR) which supply membranes).

Why markets may miss it: Water costs are small but penalties are large and often invisible in early models. Standard hydrogen project announcements mention “±10% water recycling” or “connection to grid water” in passing, but few highlight multi-year water-permitting or dedicated new treatment works. Investors drawing parallels to ethanol or ammonia might skim over feedstock water entirely. But water is no commodity where global markets will quickly expand supply—every project needs a local solution. If unchecked, the first delays will hit projects quietly (e.g. a plant sitting idle while waiting on a new filtration permit), becoming recognized only in local news, not in quarterly reports.

Key disconfirmers: We find no large-scale hydrogen plant that cranked up without addressing water limits. Inversely, where water is abundant or recycled (e.g. a coastal port using municipal desalination), supply is trivial and electric supply becomes next bottleneck. A breakthrough in direct seawater electrolysis (currently experimental (riefilt.com)) would nullify much of this constraint. Such a scenario would be signaled by pilots demonstrating stable high-grade hydrogen from sea without multi-stage RO. Another false alarm would be if the bottleneck simply shifts to once-unconsidered needs – e.g. if massive electrolyzers crowd factory parks but grid upgrades or turbine blade supply become the critical factor instead, then water would no longer derail projects. Conversely, indicators that our thesis is playing out include published abstraction-permit queues, announcements of new water-treatment BOOT contracts in the hydrogen sector (like Plug’s Mendota co-finance), or trade shows emphasizing water purity (note the Hydrogen Technology Expo panels on water).

Watch indicators: Early signals are regulatory and contractual. Check hydrogen projects’ permitting filings: if “Secured water rights” or “third-party water supply agreement” appear in filings or RFPs, the constraint is binding. Monitor regulators: e.g. Wales’ EA or local water agencies listing hydrogen in abstraction management plans. Vendor announcements matter too – new ultra-pure water systems aimed at hydrogen projects, or chemical companies boosting ion-exchange resin capacity. Also track which hydrogen facilities announce using recycled or alternative water sources (wastewater plants, desal) vs. relying on tapped municipal water. If an electrolyzer project is slowed or redesigned due to “changing water quality” or if a hydrogen hub includes a dedicated treatment plant, that’s evidence our bottleneck is in play.

alternative scenarios: 1. Water supply not limiting: Imagine a region with excess fresh water and a modular electrolyzer design that tolerates lower water quality (future AEM technologies, for instance). In that case, the water O&M issue vanishes and capital shifts to electrolyzer stacks or grid integration. We would see hydrogen ventures in that region announce no new water infrastructure and operate off existing city water without scare. A single case of a GW-scale plant built without new water permits or treatment would falsify the claim. 2. Mass recycling solves issue: If regulations or climate push every hydrogen project to recycle virtually 100% of its electrolyzer feedwater (closed-loop), then freshwater draw becomes negligible. That might happen via incentives or technology. The trigger would be policies mandating X% water recycling, or a string of projects all adopting advanced brine-RO (recapturing >90% of RO waste). The kill criterion would be if a major plant publicly claims “No net withdrawal from local supply”. 3. Early technology surprise: If scientists achieve commercially viable direct seawater H₂ without pretreatment (still a lab field), then needing RO/EDI is obsolete. A demonstration plant winning permits on that basis (citing direct SW electrolysis) would disprove the core need for ultrapure feed. Watch for journal breakthroughs or pilots showing that. Each scenario would dramatically reduce the perceived O&M value of pure water systems, shifting investor focus to the next tallest hurdle (power cables, catalysts, etc.).

In sum, the big picture: green hydrogen’s Achilles’ heel may well be water infrastructure. Without conscious planning around it – through permits, purification, or recycling – projects risk hidden delays or higher long-run costs. Investors and policymakers should watch beyond stack cost curves: track water availability, capital flow for treatment plants, and the literature on electrolyzer water tolerance. If future reports show fewer water-related announcements or new solution partners (like Plug’s Mendota or partnerships with water utilities), the issue may be easing. But until then, treat freshwater as the unsung commodity that could still stall a 21st-century energy transition.
