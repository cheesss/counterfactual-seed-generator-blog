The Engine Shop Is the New Gate on Airline Capacity

Airline capacity is usually discussed as an airframe problem. Boeing has quality and certification constraints. Airbus has a deep backlog and a slower-than-promised path to higher A320 output.

By Bottleneck Briefs · Published 2026-06-22 · 11 min read

Airline capacity is usually discussed as an airframe problem. Boeing has quality and certification constraints. Airbus has a deep backlog and a slower-than-promised path to higher A320 output. Airports, pilots, air-traffic control, and geopolitical closures all limit the number of flights the system can absorb. Those explanations are real. They are also incomplete.

A modern airline does not fly an order book. It flies serviceable aircraft with serviceable engines. In this cycle, the practical gate on capacity may be less about how many narrowbodies Airbus or Boeing can assemble and more about how quickly the engine ecosystem can return a grounded aircraft to revenue service.

The bottleneck sits in the certified engine-aftermarket stack: shop slots, spare engines, turbine and compressor parts, repair instructions, licensed MRO providers, used serviceable material, PMA and DER alternatives, test cells, trained mechanics, and regulator-approved inspection workscopes. That sounds like a maintenance department problem. It is not. It is a capacity, pricing, and risk-transfer problem for the entire aviation system.

The clearest market signal is that engines are being valued in ways that invert the usual aircraft logic. Business Insider reported that nearly new Spirit Airlines A320neos were being dismantled for parts, with scarce GTF engines leasing for about $400,000 per month as spare assets. A young aircraft normally has value because it can fly for years. In this case, the parts and propulsion system could be more valuable than the intact frame.

That is the shape of a real bottleneck. The scarce item is not just the aircraft. It is the combination of engine serial number, spare availability, repair slot, approved parts, proprietary instructions, and return-to-service paperwork.

The cost is already visible

IATA and Oliver Wyman estimated that commercial aircraft supply-chain failures could cost airlines more than $11 billion in 2025. The breakdown matters: about $4.2 billion from delayed fuel savings, $3.1 billion from additional maintenance, $2.6 billion from excess engine leasing, and $1.4 billion from inventory holding. Their report also says engine shop visits that historically took 30 to 60 days are stretching upward of 300 days in some cases, while 75% of surveyed MRO respondents saw engine and APU turnaround times increase over the prior year.

Those numbers connect the industrial bottleneck to the income statement. A delayed engine repair does not simply produce an operational inconvenience. It can delay fuel savings from newer aircraft, force airlines to lease spare engines at elevated rates, keep more inventory on hand, and push carriers toward compensation claims or litigation.

The new-engine cycle made this more fragile. Airlines wanted A320neo, 737 MAX, A220, 787, A350, and other newer fleets because fuel efficiency mattered after the post-pandemic traffic recovery. But newer engines are not simple commodity replacements for older ones. They bring new materials, hotter operating conditions, tighter tolerances, proprietary repair ecosystems, and inspection regimes that can pull aircraft into the shop earlier than planned. When a new platform has a durability or manufacturing problem, the issue does not stay inside the engineering department. It competes with new-engine production, spare-engine pools, and the MRO network needed to keep existing fleets flying.

Pratt turned metallurgy into fleet availability risk

Pratt & Whitney's geared turbofan issue is the most visible example. RTX disclosed in September 2023 that a powder-metal manufacturing problem would require roughly 600 to 700 incremental PW1100G shop visits from 2023 through 2026, beyond prior expectations. RTX described accelerated removals, more aircraft on ground, additional maintenance capacity, higher part output, and a $3 billion to $3.5 billion pre-tax operating profit impact.

That path is important: a manufacturing defect became service bulletins, then inspections, then airworthiness directives, then aircraft-on-ground counts, compensation packages, and airline strategy. FAA directives covering affected International Aero Engines parts show how the regulatory layer can harden an engineering problem into a required maintenance workload.

Wizz Air shows how this becomes a growth constraint. Reuters reported that Wizz had 33 aircraft grounded at the end of December 2025, down from 40 a year earlier. Management targeted an average of 20 to 25 grounded aircraft in the following fiscal year and expected all aircraft back in service by the end of calendar 2027. Yet Wizz also selected Pratt & Whitney engines for 177 additional A321neo aircraft, taking its GTF-powered aircraft order total to 453, alongside a support package and long-term service agreement.

That is not irrational loyalty to a troubled supplier. It is what happens when the scarce asset is not just an engine but access to the support system around the engine. A carrier exposed to one engine family may have little choice but to deepen the relationship if that is how it secures repair capacity, compensation, and future supply.

ITA Airways is a second useful case. Reuters reported that the airline was considering legal action against Pratt & Whitney after engine faults grounded almost 20% of its 80-aircraft fleet, with damage estimated around EUR50 million.

This is where maintenance stops being a back-office function. The engine ecosystem becomes a contract, litigation, and bargaining system. Airlines bought promised fuel efficiency and reliability. OEMs and service networks now have to allocate constrained capacity while preserving customer relationships and absorbing some of the economic damage.

The airframer bottleneck is tied to the engine shop

Airbus has its own production and supplier constraints, but engine availability now sits inside that delivery story. Airbus said in February 2026 that it expected about 870 commercial aircraft deliveries for the year and softened its A320 ramp target to 70 to 75 aircraft per month by the end of 2027, stabilizing at rate 75 thereafter. Reuters and other coverage tied that adjustment partly to Pratt & Whitney engine delays.

That is the operating mechanism investors often miss. Repair demand and new production are not separate worlds. They can pull on overlapping parts, technicians, engineering support, supplier capacity, and OEM attention. If a fleet issue requires more shop visits, more parts, and more compensation, it can also affect the ability to supply new engines to airframers on schedule.

The CFM LEAP side of the market is different, but it points to the same structural question. LEAP has a huge installed base and a major backlog. As the fleet ages, shop visits are expected to rise sharply toward the end of the decade. CFM has shipped more than 1,200 LEAP-1A high-pressure-turbine durability kits and is rolling out maturity upgrades intended to increase time on wing, especially in severe environments.

The industry is expanding capacity in response. Safran opened a 45,000-square-meter LEAP MRO center in Hyderabad targeting 300 LEAP shop visits per year and training more than 100 Indian technicians and engineers annually. IAG and CFM signed a LEAP Premier MRO license that positions Iberia's La Munoza engine shop near Madrid as a European LEAP hub, with initial inductions planned for the first quarter of 2027. These are not generic growth announcements. They are attempts to build the physical, human, and contractual infrastructure needed to prevent the next maintenance wave from becoming the next capacity shock.

Who controls the constraint

The control points are more distributed than a simple "engine maker wins" story. Pratt & Whitney controls the GTF fleet-management plan and much of the support network. GE Aerospace and Safran, through CFM, shape the LEAP ecosystem. GE Aerospace's CEO told Reuters that the company earns more than 70% of commercial engine revenue from parts and services, which explains why airlines watch aftermarket pricing power so closely.

But the pressure valves include many other actors: Safran sites in India and Mexico, Iberia Maintenance, Delta TechOps, MTU Maintenance, Lufthansa Technik, StandardAero, Eagle Services Asia, Korean Air, IHI, China Airlines, used-serviceable-material traders, teardown firms, PMA suppliers, DER repair developers, spare-engine lessors, and regulators such as FAA and EASA.

The legal and regulatory architecture matters because capacity is not created by buildings alone. A shop needs manuals, repair instructions, approved parts, test cells, trained labor, data, and regulator-accepted procedures. IATA and CFM's pro-competitive engine-maintenance agreement, renewed through 2033, is relevant for that reason. It addresses access to manuals, repair instructions, third-party parts, and independent MRO options.

That agreement does not make the bottleneck disappear. It defines how the industry will fight over relief. If OEM-controlled parts and repairs remain the choke point, pricing power stays concentrated. If independent MRO, PMA parts, DER repairs, and used serviceable material gain wider acceptance, the bottleneck can loosen and value can migrate toward alternative providers.

What would prove this wrong

The counterargument is strong. The system is not standing still. Pratt & Whitney is investing more than $100 million across U.S. MRO sites in Irving, West Palm Beach, and Springdale, expanding used-serviceable-material stock, GTF capacity, engine-case repairs, and additive-manufacturing repair capability. Pratt says its GTF network includes 21 global engine centers and about 40 component repair facilities. Eagle Services Asia expanded GTF capacity in Singapore. Safran is expanding Hyderabad and Queretaro. CFM's durability work is designed to improve time on wing. Wizz's grounded count has already moved down from the prior year.

If turnaround times normalize, spare-engine lease rates fall, groundings keep declining, new MRO sites reach planned throughput, and durability fixes reduce early removals, this becomes a painful catch-up cycle rather than a persistent industry bottleneck.

It could also be overshadowed by other constraints. Boeing quality, Airbus supplier limits, seats, interiors, avionics, airport slots, pilot availability, and demand softness can all become the binding issue in a given year. The engine shop is not the only gate on airline capacity. The question is narrower: when the system is already tight, does engine return-to-service capacity increasingly set the tempo?

What to watch

The indicators are concrete. Watch aircraft-on-ground counts by engine family, not just total fleet size. Watch shop-visit turnaround time and whether it moves back toward 60 to 90 days. Watch spare-engine lease rates, especially for GTF and LEAP families. Watch whether young aircraft continue to be parted out. Watch FAA and EASA airworthiness directives, service bulletins, and the inspection workloads they create. Watch PMA, DER, and used-serviceable-material adoption. Watch compensation settlements and long-term service agreements. Watch whether new MRO facilities hit stated throughput without simply pulling scarce labor and parts from existing shops.

The broader lesson is that aviation capacity is becoming an aftermarket story. In an easier cycle, investors can focus on deliveries, passenger demand, and airline pricing. In this cycle, the narrowest asset may be a serviceable engine, a qualified shop slot, a proprietary repair instruction, or a certified test cell. Airline growth is still an order-book story, but it is also an availability story. The engine shop increasingly decides how much of the order book can actually fly.
