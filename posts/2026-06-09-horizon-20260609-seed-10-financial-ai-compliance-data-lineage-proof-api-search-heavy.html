Seed Provenance

This seed is mechanically pointing at a shift from “Can we govern the model?” to “Can we reconstruct what happened, from which data, with which approvals, after the fact?” The search results do not settle that shift, but they do show three live frictions in financial AI: third-party dependence, data governance, and auditability. In a 2024 UK survey of financial firms, 33% of AI use cases were third-party implementations, 46% of firms said they only partially understood the AI technologies they used, and data management/governance was called out as a key concern rather than something distinctly AI-specific. (bankofengland.co.uk)

The seed’s “bottleneck candidate” therefore looks less like a single law or single control and more like an interface problem: existing model-risk frameworks are visible, but the traceability layer may be where implementation effort accumulates once AI touches compliance, AML, fraud, lending, or customer operations. That is still a hypothesis, not a conclusion. (bankofengland.co.uk)

Search Findings

The strongest near-term evidence for a data-lineage / evidence-trail bottleneck comes from supervisors and rulemakers repeatedly naming traceability, logs, and data lineage as core controls. The Basel Committee’s BCBS 239 update says recent outreach sessions focused on banks’ data management and risk management challenges, explicitly including data lineage, governance of risk-data-aggregation activities, and emerging technology. It also says some banks have extended BCBS 239 into broader enterprise-wide data governance frameworks, reflecting recognition of data as a strategic asset. (bis.org)

The EU AI Act goes further on the evidence side. For high-risk AI systems, Article 12 requires automatic logging over the system’s lifetime so traceability is possible; Article 19 requires providers to keep those logs for at least six months, and financial institutions subject to Union financial-services governance rules must maintain the logs as part of their documentation. The AI Act Service Desk summary also says logs should capture events relevant to risk identification, post-market monitoring, and operation monitoring. (ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu)

The ECB’s 2024 financial-stability article makes the same point from a different angle: the overall impact of AI on finance depends on how challenges related to data, model development, and deployment are addressed. It also says AI can be hard for humans to comprehend and reconstruct, and that risks may be even larger for proprietary and non-auditable systems. That language is directly relevant to the seed’s “audit evidence” branch. (ecb.europa.eu)

At the same time, classic model governance is still alive and structurally important. The OCC’s 2026 revised model-risk guidance emphasizes model development, testing, validation, monitoring, and governance/controls; but it also says generative AI and agentic AI are not within the scope of that guidance and that the guidance is non-prescriptive. That means the supervisory frame for AI is still evolving, and the older model-risk toolkit is not yet the whole answer. (occ.gov)

The UK survey points to the same ambiguity. Firms reported that the largest regulatory constraint to AI use is data protection and privacy, followed by resilience, cybersecurity, third-party rules, and Consumer Duty; the largest non-regulatory constraint is safety/security/robustness of models, followed by talent and skills, while transparency/explainability also matters. So the bottleneck does not look like “model governance versus nothing else”; it looks like multiple constraints stacking, with data governance and evidence capture becoming especially salient as AI matures. (bankofengland.co.uk)

Counterfactual Scenarios

If AI stays mostly in low-materiality productivity uses—summarization, drafting, internal search, workflow support—then model validation may remain visible but not decisive, and the biggest compliance burden may continue to be generic privacy, security, and change management rather than bespoke audit-evidence infrastructure. That scenario is compatible with the survey finding that 62% of AI use cases were rated low materiality and only 2% were fully autonomous. (bankofengland.co.uk)

If financial firms move AI deeper into risk, compliance, fraud, credit, and customer decisioning, then the burden likely shifts toward “can we explain and replay the decision path?” rather than “is the model broadly documented?” The EU AI Act’s logging/recordkeeping obligations and the BCBS 239 focus on lineage are consistent with that path. The question is whether firms treat those obligations as a narrow high-risk compliance layer or as a broader operating standard. (ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu)

If third-party and foundation-model usage keeps rising, the bottleneck may shift again—from internal model validation to supplier evidence and integration controls. The UK survey shows third-party implementations are already common, partial understanding is widespread, and third-party dependencies are expected to increase as a risk over the next three years. In that world, the issue may be less “build a perfect in-house validation stack” and more “obtain auditable artifacts from vendors and preserve them internally.” (bankofengland.co.uk)

If regulators remain mostly satisfied with risk-based governance and do not require per-decision reconstruction except for high-risk systems, then the lineage/audit-evidence market may grow slower than the seed implies. The OCC guidance is non-prescriptive and the AI Act’s strongest logging rules are scoped to high-risk systems, so the generalized “audit evidence store” thesis could be too broad unless specific use cases trigger it. (occ.gov)

Bottleneck Map

One working map is:

1. Model governance layer Questions here: Is the system validated, tested, monitored, and assigned to an accountable owner? Are third-party models reviewed? Is explainability sufficient for the use case? The UK survey suggests many firms already have accountable persons and multiple AI governance components, which implies this layer is becoming normalized rather than novel. (bankofengland.co.uk)

2. Data-lineage / audit-evidence layer Questions here: Which source fed which output? Which transformation or prompt version was used? Which logs were retained, by whom, and for how long? BCBS 239, the EU AI Act, and the ECB all point to traceability, logging, and reconstructability as live supervisory concerns. This is the layer that looks most operationally burdensome when AI output must be defended later. (bis.org)

3. Operating-environment layer Questions here: Do privacy, cybersecurity, resilience, and third-party rules block deployment before either governance layer matters? The BoE/FCA survey says these are major constraints, which means the bottleneck may sometimes sit outside AI-specific controls entirely. (bankofengland.co.uk)

The current search evidence suggests the data-lineage / audit-evidence layer is the best candidate bottleneck for regulated or semi-regulated AI use cases, but only as an inference from overlapping signals, not as a settled finding. The key reason is that model governance is already familiar, whereas reconstructable evidence across data, prompts, outputs, human approvals, and vendor dependencies is still being spelled out in supervisory language. (bis.org)

Counterevidence And Falsifiers

A first falsifier is if firms can keep most AI in low-materiality, human-supervised workflows and satisfy regulators with ordinary model-risk documentation, privacy controls, and change-management records. The BoE/FCA survey gives this scenario real weight: most use cases were low materiality, only a minority were high materiality, and only 2% were fully autonomous. (bankofengland.co.uk)

A second falsifier is if supervisors continue to focus more on validation and outcomes than on decision replay. The OCC’s revised model-risk guidance still centers model development, validation, monitoring, and governance. If that remains the dominant supervisory template, then lineage may be a supporting control rather than the main bottleneck. (occ.gov)

A third falsifier is if the binding legal regime is mostly scoped high-risk AI law rather than broad financial-services practice. The EU AI Act’s strongest logging duties apply to high-risk systems; outside that category, the compliance burden may be lighter or indirect. If financial firms can route most use cases outside high-risk classifications, the “audit evidence store” thesis weakens. (ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu)

A fourth falsifier is if the hard problem turns out to be supplier control, not traceability. The UK survey’s third-party figures and the ECB’s concentration concerns suggest that vendor dependence could be the true choke point; in that world, the issue is less evidence storage and more contractual access, model portability, and operational resilience. (bankofengland.co.uk)

Candidate Exposures To Investigate Later

  • GRC and model-risk platforms that extend from validation into evidence management.
  • Data catalog / lineage / observability tools that can connect source data to AI outputs.
  • Immutable logging, archive, and records-management infrastructure for prompts, outputs, approvals, and exceptions.
  • Third-party AI governance tooling for vendor due diligence, artifact collection, and model-change tracking.
  • Identity, access, and workflow systems that can prove who approved what, when, and under which policy.
  • Compliance and e-discovery layers that sit between everyday operations and regulator-facing reconstruction.

These are not recommendations; they are candidate surfaces for later mapping if the seed’s bottleneck thesis keeps holding up.
