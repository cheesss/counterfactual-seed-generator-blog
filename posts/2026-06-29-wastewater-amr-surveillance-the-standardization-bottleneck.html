Wastewater AMR Surveillance: the Standardization Bottleneck

Post-COVID, sewage surveillance for resistance is scaling fast. The block is not pipes or sequencers — it is incomparable lab methods. Different labs use different assays, gene targets, and pipelines. Without harmonized protocols and reference standards, results don't line up.

By Bottleneck Briefs · Published 2026-06-29 · 8 min read

Wastewater-based epidemiology had its breakout during COVID-19, and governments and investors now want to point the same sewers at antimicrobial resistance. The pipes, samplers, and sequencers are largely in place, and the political will and funding have followed. The stumbling block sits somewhere less obvious. Laboratories run different qPCR assays, chase different gene targets, and process their sequencing through incompatible pipelines, so a resistance-gene count from one city cannot be lined up against another's. Unless sewage analyses converge on common protocols and certified reference standards, the effort produces a torrent of numbers no one can compare — a validation gap, not a hardware gap. The question that decides whether any of it becomes usable public-health infrastructure is therefore institutional: who writes the standard, who accredits the protocol, and who supplies the certified reference materials.

Regulators and investors often assume that deploying more sensors and sequencers will scale wastewater AMR surveillance. The inescapable issue is more subtle: metrology and standardization. A 2022 survey of US water studies found that “numerous quality assurance and other aspects of protocols” still need alignment to make gene counts reproducible and comparable (www.tandfonline.com). Another review bluntly lists “lack of standardized protocols” as a core constraint for wastewater AR surveillance and calls for harmonized sampling, processing and reporting (www.sciencedirect.com). Similarly, Borchardt et al. (2021) observed that many environmental qPCR studies omit basic controls, complicating cross-study comparisons. Their EMMI guidelines outline key quality checks and terminology so that different labs’ results can be merged (pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov). In short, the scientific community is already working toward standard targets and controls, precisely because unreproducible methods would block any cohesive monitoring effort.

This gap emerges in real data. For example, a city-wide pilot in Alicante (Spain) sequenced ARGs in hospital and WWTP effluent while simultaneously counting two specific resistance genes (sul2, tetW) by digital PCR (doaj.org). Both methods saw the same hot spots (highest ARGs at the hospital inlet) (doaj.org), but the numeric answers differed drastically. Digital PCR readouts were in the thousands of gene copies per ng DNA, whereas the metagenomic pipeline reported only hundreds of hits per gigabase. Notably, a direct correlation between the two methods was essentially zero (r²<0.4) (doaj.org). Without an agreed way to convert between “copies/ng” and “ARGs/Gb”, one lab’s data cannot substitute for another’s, making accidentals like random gene families or sequencing depth drive the story instead of true epidemiological signal.

Named examples: The U.S. CDC launched its National Wastewater Surveillance System (NWSS) for SARS-CoV-2 and explicitly plans to extend it “beyond SARS-CoV-2” to other pathogens, including AMR (www.sciencedirect.com). But the data NWSS collects can only be useful if protocols are common. On the research front, Harwood & Pruden (2022) combed through 117 studies and identified the five most common qPCR targets (sul1, tetA, intI1 and clinically important genes vanA, blaCTX-M) to recommend as a standard panel (www.tandfonline.com). These gene markers have broad consensus utility, but only if every lab uses them under similar conditions. California has already started pilot programs, ordering tests of recycled wastewater for AR genes (Mandate by State Water Board) (www.tandfonline.com), effectively betting that data from million-dollar qPCR or sequencing runs will be interpretable across sites. Without concrete guidelines, each municipality could be measuring a slightly different spectrum of genes or using a different calibration, making comparisons as futile as comparing apples to oranges.

The column’s thesis in capital-markets terms is that the hidden constraint is not the obvious resource shortage (like sequencing machines or samplers), but the need for labs and agencies to align their playbooks. This is very much a “validation-evidence” bottleneck: you can build testing factories quickly, but they can’t output credible signals until everyone agrees on what a test result even means. The deepest choke point is societal, not physical – who builds the agreed standards, who accredits the protocols, and who provides the certified reference materials? These might involve bodies like WHO/GLASS, regulatory agencies or standard organizations (ISO/ASTM) setting official method documents. Without those, individual test facilities remain islands of data.

Why might markets miss this? The headlines are full of “more surveillance funding” and new sequencing contracts, which look like spending paths (capital flow through equipment vendors and test labs). But that money is wasted if the data are incomparable. Investors and policymakers focus on output (e.g. terabases of sequencing) rather than throughput standards. A $10 million sequencing grant or a fleet of qPCR robots sounds impressive, but if there’s no metrology lab to intercalibrate those machines, we’ll just have mountains of unstandardized numbers. In effect, value capture in this avenue lies with whoever controls the validation process: accreditation bodies, proficiency-test providers, or consensus protocol committees – not with raw hardware suppliers per se. The risk is akin to software incompatibility in an IT upgrade: you can buy the fastest CPU, but if your software requires a specific architecture, it won’t run faster. Here the “software” is lab protocol harmonization.

Counterarguments: One might contend that rapid progress in DNA tech will soon render manual calibration moot. For example, if portable sequencers (Oxford Nanopore, etc.) become cheap and ubiquitous, maybe standard PCR panels are obsolete. Yet even those outputs need standardized analysis pipelines. Or some may say that wide variations in microbiome data are acceptable noise, as long as trends can be observed. But if public health decisions are made from raw ARG counts, a consistent baseline is essential. A potential falsifier: demonstration projects that successfully compare AR prevalence across regions without standardized protocols. To date, such consensus is lacking. If say, the EU’s RE-SURE project or WHO pilots publish interlaboratory comparisons showing tight agreement using varied methods, that would blow up the current thesis. (We found no evidence of any such convergence yet.)

Watch indicators: The key tag-the-switch event would be issuing of an official methodology. For instance, if WHO or an ISO committee release a certified qPCR kit with reference reagents, or if CDC publishes standard operating protocols for wastewater AMR under NWSS, that would signal relief. Another signal: the creation of inter-lab proficiency-testing schemes (like the API’s ring tests) that labs enroll in. Absent those, look at the variance in reported gene levels over time from the same site – if different surveys of the same wastewater show wildly different AR levels, the bottleneck remains. In contrast, narrowing reproducibility gaps across pilot sites would indicate progress.

In sum, the overlooked bottleneck is the quality assurance layer. To scale AMR wastewater surveillance, we need more than pumps and sequencers; we need universal tests. Those who control the normalization standards – from academic consortiums to international agencies – will effectively control the utility of all downstream data. A decentralized scramble without such alignment will produce only disjointed measurements, akin to having numerous weather stations that each report temperature in different units. Markets and funds must watch not only lab headcounts or chip shipments, but the emergence of shared protocols and reference materials. If such measures never materialize, policymakers will waste funds on an “arm’s race” of testing without a coherent reference frame – a classic waterfall scenario where the dam gate is closed by inconsistent calibration rather than lack of water flow.
