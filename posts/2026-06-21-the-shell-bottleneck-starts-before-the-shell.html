The Shell Bottleneck Starts Before the Shell

Western rearmament looks like a factory problem because the visible object is a shell body. Governments announce new machining lines, load-assemble-pack capacity, and monthly output targets.

Western rearmament looks like a factory problem because the visible object is a shell body. Governments announce new machining lines, load-assemble-pack capacity, and monthly output targets. The harder constraint may sit earlier in the chain, inside the chemical plants that make a complete round possible.

The U.S. Army describes a complete 155mm shot as a system: fuze, primer, loaded projectile, and propelling charge. A steel casing without explosive fill, propellant, primer, and fuze does not solve an artillery shortage.

That distinction matters because energetic materials do not scale like ordinary metal parts. TNT, nitrocellulose, RDX, IMX fills, propelling charges, acid recovery, wastewater treatment, hazardous-waste handling, explosives safety, air permits, and local approvals all sit upstream of the round. A country can fund more shells and still wait on nitration capacity.

The first tell is TNT

The United States had to reconstitute domestic TNT production. In December 2024, the Army awarded REPKON USA Defense a contract ceiling of $435 million to design, build, and commission a TNT production facility in Graham, Kentucky. The Army said the award would restore U.S. TNT production for the first time in decades and that the existing U.S. TNT supply chain relied entirely on overseas sources.

A GAO protest record shows the scale. The Army's 2023 sources-sought notice targeted a continental U.S. facility capable of 5 million to 15 million pounds of TNT per year. The preferred schedule called for construction and commissioning within 36 months, with a 48-month outside target after award.

That is the shape of the constraint. The bottleneck does not clear when a government signs a check. It clears when a chemical plant exists, passes its permitting path, reaches qualification, handles waste streams, and produces material at usable quality and volume.

The arithmetic explains why this matters. Defense News reported that a senior Army acquisition official identified enough explosives as the biggest concern for the 100,000-round-per-month 155mm target. The article estimated that a shell containing roughly 22 pounds of explosive would imply about 26.4 million pounds of energetics per year at that output level.

The plant is also a permit stack

Holston Army Ammunition Plant shows the mechanism in operating form. BAE Systems runs the Tennessee site under a long-term Army contract. The plant is being modernized with nitration and weak acetic-acid recovery infrastructure, and a supplemental environmental assessment for IMX expansion tied the project to urgent Department of Defense demand and surge requirements.

The same Holston record discusses waste streams, air quality, hazardous waste, industrial wastewater, static detonation chamber permitting, and flashing-furnace permitting. That is not administrative noise. It is the physical boundary around energetic-materials capacity.

Radford Army Ammunition Plant shows the propellant side. The Army describes nitrocellulose as a critical ingredient in all propellants. Modernization materials name a new nitrocellulose production facility, and Joint Munitions Command materials describe a decade of environmental investment, including reductions in open-burning-ground waste and plans for an energetic-waste incinerator with pollution controls.

The pattern is consistent: a permitted nitration line, acid recovery system, waste-treatment path, and qualified operating base can become more valuable than another press release about shell-body throughput.

Europe is running the same experiment

The European Union's ASAP program framed ammunition expansion around bottlenecks, not around shell bodies alone. The Commission named explosives, propelling powder, shells, missiles, testing, and raw materials as target categories and allocated about EUR 500 million across the program.

Chemring Nobel received support to more than double explosives production in Norway. Rheinmetall's EU-supported projects touched ammunition, powder, and explosives across several countries. These grants make sense only if policymakers see chemical throughput as a pacing item.

Poland's Nitro-Chem gives the import-bridge case. Reuters reported that the company signed a 1.2 billion zloty agreement, roughly $310 million, to supply 18,000 tonnes of TNT to the U.S. market over 2027 to 2029. Reuters described Nitro-Chem as Europe's largest TNT producer.

Sweden Ballistics gives the greenfield-permit case. Reuters reported that Sweden's Land and Environmental Court approved the company's plan to build a TNT factory in Nora targeting 4,500 tonnes per year by 2028. The court decision is the industrial event. Without that approval, the capacity stays theoretical.

The real unit is the complete shot

The useful question is not how many shell bodies a country can cut, forge, or machine. The useful question is how many complete shots can move through explosives, propellant, fuzes, primers, LAP, inspection, storage, and transport at the same time.

That creates a different map of exposed assets. A permitted TNT plant matters. A nitrocellulose line matters. Acid recovery matters. Hazardous-waste treatment matters. A GOCO operator with modernization experience matters. A supplier that already holds qualifications and environmental permissions has a head start that budget cannot instantly buy.

The counterargument keeps the thesis honest

Energetics may not remain the binding constraint. Fuzes, primers, LAP lines, gun barrels, quality assurance, skilled labor, transport rules, budget durability, and demand visibility can all slow output. The Army's own expansion plan treats 155mm as a full-system problem. Europe funds shells and missiles as well as explosives and powder.

The test is simple. If complete-shot output rises with shell-body capacity, the chemical stack becomes one constraint among many. If shell bodies and LAP lines expand faster than TNT, nitrocellulose, IMX, propellant, and waste-permitted throughput, the bottleneck migrates upstream.

That upstream layer is less visible because it looks like chemistry, environmental control, and qualification paperwork. It is still industrial power. In an artillery rearmament cycle, the decisive factory may be the one that can legally and safely make the energetic material before the shell ever reaches assembly.

What To Watch

  • TNT domestic-production awards.
  • Nitrocellulose and propellant modernization programs.
  • IMX/RDX/NTO capacity expansions.
  • Acid recovery and nitration facility investments.
  • NEPA, RCRA, air, water, hazardous-waste, and local court records.
  • Long-term offtake contracts for bulk energetics.
  • Government-owned contractor-operated ammunition-plant modernization.
  • Delays between announced shell-body capacity and complete-shot capacity.
