Antibiotic Subscription Models Create A Reserved-Drug Access And Stewardship Data Bottleneck

Antibiotic subscription models are usually described as a cure for broken antibiotic economics. That description is accurate, but incomplete. The economic problem is real. New antibiotics are often most valuable when they are not used.

By Bottleneck Briefs · Published 2026-06-29 · 5 min read

Antibiotic subscription models are usually described as a cure for broken antibiotic economics. That description is accurate, but incomplete.

The economic problem is real. New antibiotics are often most valuable when they are not used. Health systems reserve them for resistant infections, stewardship programs limit prescribing, and hospitals avoid broad uptake unless a patient has no suitable alternatives. That logic protects public health, but it also destroys the sales model that normally funds drug development. Delinked subscription contracts try to solve this by paying companies for access and value rather than volume [S1], [S3].

The UK has gone furthest. NHS England contracted for cefiderocol and ceftazidime-avibactam, manufactured by Shionogi and Pfizer respectively, under a subscription-style model. These are not ordinary antibiotics. Both are Reserve drugs, intended for serious infections caused by resistant gram-negative bacteria, and their use is restricted by specialist advice, susceptibility or resistance-mechanism testing, and the requirement that no suitable alternatives exist [S6], [S10], [S11], [S31].

That creates a second bottleneck. Once the company is paid, the decisive work moves into the hospital. A patient must be identified quickly. The organism or resistance mechanism must be confirmed or strongly suspected. A Microbiology or infectious-disease specialist must agree. The drug must be on formulary, available through pharmacy, and usable in the patient's care setting. The case must then be captured in surveillance or registry systems so the health system can learn from rare use [S10], [S11], [S12], [S13], [S20].

This is not necessarily bad. Reserve antibiotics should be controlled. WHO says Reserve antibiotics should be available but limited to highly specific patients and closely monitored [S30]. The bottleneck becomes dangerous only if the system pays for national availability but underfunds the local machinery required for real access.

The UK model shows both sides. On the positive side, NHS England expanded the program after the pilot, and the 2024 model includes supply obligations, stewardship commitments, performance reporting, resistance monitoring, and contractual requirements to maintain availability [S3], [S5], [S9]. On the negative side, NICE and later analyses repeatedly note uncertainty about eligible patient numbers, limited clinical evidence, imperfect surveillance data, and reliance on susceptibility evidence where patient-outcome data are sparse [S14], [S15], [S19]. A pharmacist survey found strong support for the model but continuing concerns about cost, local formulary decisions, aseptic preparation, and reporting workflows [S20].

The United States is approaching the same issue through the PASTEUR Act. The reintroduced bill would use federal subscription-style payments for qualifying antimicrobials, with annual payments described as USD 75 million to USD 300 million for up to 10 years, while requiring supply, stewardship, education, resistance reporting, manufacturing, and post-market commitments [S21], [S22]. Critics argue that pull incentives must be tied more tightly to access, affordability, diagnostics, stewardship, and patient-relevant evidence [S23], [S24], [S40].

The most useful interpretation is that antibiotic subscriptions do not eliminate bottlenecks. They relocate them. The manufacturer revenue bottleneck is relieved, but the Reserve-drug access bottleneck and the stewardship-data bottleneck become central. A good model must therefore pay not only for the molecule, but for the operating system around the molecule: diagnostics, specialist approval pathways, local pharmacy capacity, registry integration, resistance surveillance, and budget-neutral hospital access.

The policy test is simple. If a subscription model can show that eligible patients receive the drug quickly, that use remains appropriately restricted, that resistance and outcomes are captured, and that manufacturers maintain reliable supply, then it is more than an R&D subsidy. If it cannot show those things, it may fix the launch economics while leaving the clinical access problem unresolved.

In brief

  • Delinked antibiotic subscriptions fix a real market failure: Reserve antibiotics are clinically valuable partly because they are used sparingly, which makes volume-based revenue structurally weak [S1], [S3], [S38], [S39].
  • The UK model is the strongest public case study: cefiderocol and ceftazidime-avibactam were contracted through subscription-style arrangements and are restricted by specialist advice, susceptibility or resistance testing, and "no suitable alternatives" criteria [S6], [S10], [S11].
  • The access bottleneck is operational: formulary status, diagnostics, Microbiology and infectious-disease signoff, local budget effects, aseptic preparation, OPAT feasibility, and pharmacy release can matter as much as national procurement [S20], [S36], [S37].
  • The data bottleneck is structural: NICE relies on UKAR, Blueteq or related monitoring, surveillance systems, and post-launch evidence to understand rare use, outcomes, resistance, safety, and resource use [S12], [S13], [S16], [S17].
  • Disconfirming evidence is meaningful: NHS England's model includes supply, stewardship, performance reporting, resistance monitoring, and delinked sales incentives; it is not merely a blank check for manufacturers [S3], [S5], [S9].
  • The unresolved issue is whether subscriptions finance the full access-and-learning system or only the national right to use a drug.

Key figures

  • UK subscription model value bands for England in the 2024 guidance: GBP 20 million per year, GBP 15 million per year, GBP 10 million per year, and GBP 5 million per year, depending on assessed product value category [S3].
  • NHS England said the first two UK subscription drugs could help about 1,700 patients per year with severe drug-resistant infections [S6].
  • The 2024 UK model describes an initial 3-year contract, with extension periods up to a maximum of 15 years or the remaining exclusivity period [S3].
  • The pilot contracts for cefiderocol and ceftazidime-avibactam were described as fixed annual payments, initially 3 years, with extensions possible in the pilot framework [S7], [S14], [S15].
  • The NHS consultation analysis reported 49 responses, 95.5 percent agreement with the overall purpose, more than 70 percent support for the procurement process, about 80 percent agreement with WHO-priority-pathogen eligibility, and about 60 percent agreement with supply and stewardship performance indicators [S4].
  • The pharmacist survey included 43 respondents from NHS acute trusts, a 32.8 percent response rate; 88.4 percent supported the subscription principle or saw positive impact, 67.4 percent still considered cost significant, and 83.7 percent identified sterile or aseptic preparation as a significant limitation [S20].
  • UKAR reports more than 1,100 patients across 60 sites, with recruitment ongoing [S17].
  • PASTEUR Act public descriptions cite subscription-style annual payments of USD 75 million to USD 300 million per year for up to 10 years [S21], [S22].
  • The AMR Action Fund describes itself as a USD 1 billion initiative aiming to bring 2 to 4 new antibiotics to patients by 2030 [S34].
  • A GARDP Viewpoint describes a SECURE feasibility estimate of 33 percent lower procurement costs and USD 20 million to give 100 million people access to a 10-antibiotic portfolio for 5 years; this is a feasibility estimate, not observed implementation performance [S29].
