Battery Gigafactory Wastewater And Water Reuse Permits Become A Manufacturing Constraint

Battery factories are starting to meet a bottleneck that looks less glamorous than dry rooms, formation capacity, or cathode chemistry: water. The issue is not just whether a plant can buy enough water.

By Bottleneck Briefs · Published 2026-06-29 · 5 min read

In brief

Battery factories are starting to meet a bottleneck that looks less glamorous than dry rooms, formation capacity, or cathode chemistry: water. The issue is not just whether a plant can buy enough water. It is whether it can discharge, reuse, concentrate, haul, or crystallize what comes out of the factory without exceeding local limits or delaying ramp-up.

The constraint is uneven. Cell assembly plants can be manageable when water and wastewater are engineered early. The harder cases are EV plants with metal finishing, lithium refining, cathode and precursor cathode material plants, battery recycling, and any project relying on a local treatment plant that was not built for high-salt, high-metal, high-conductivity, or emerging-fluorinated waste streams.

Water has entered the battery-factory constraint stack through the side door. It rarely appears first in investor presentations, but it shows up in permits, consent orders, water authority letters, discharge limits, appeal decisions, and engineering packages. The emerging pattern is simple: battery factories can be built faster than local water and wastewater systems can be permitted, financed, tested, and commissioned.

The Georgia case is the most operationally vivid. Hyundai's EV plant began generating industrial wastewater before the new North Bryan County WRF was ready. Savannah's Travis Field facility stopped receiving the wastewater after reported exceedances for copper, zinc, and conductivity. The plant shifted to off-site hauling, and state regulators later settled alleged wastewater-disposal violations. The lesson is not that Hyundai cannot solve wastewater. It is that a factory can enter production while the water system needed for stable ramp is still catching up. Sources: [S5], [S6], [S13], [S14].

In Germany, Tesla's Berlin project demonstrates the land-use version of the problem. Water protection areas, water-supply contracts, process-water recycling, and battery-cell expansion planning all became intertwined. The project continued and later received expansion approval, so the evidence is not a permanent blockage. The evidence is that water can reshape the permitted geometry and phasing of an industrial site. Sources: [S16], [S17], [S18], [S20].

The hardest chemistry sits upstream of cell assembly. pCAM and CAM plants generate salty streams that can include sodium sulfate, lithium, nickel, cobalt, manganese, high pH, high TDS, and low-value salts with limited resale outlets. BASF's Harjavalta case in Finland shows that sulfate treatment and certainty over wastewater routing can be a condition for starting operations. South Korea's new brine-wastewater rules show the same pressure at national scale: regulators are trying to make salt and ecotoxicity reviews more predictable because secondary-battery brine was creating uncertainty under conventional freshwater toxicity frameworks. Sources: [S28], [S29], [S30], [S31].

PFAS is the long-tail risk. The current permitting system is still catching up to battery-related fluorinated compounds. EPA has asked states to use NPDES and pretreatment authorities to monitor and reduce PFAS at source. Scientific papers now connect lithium-ion battery components, recycling materials, landfill leachate, and some manufacturing-adjacent environments to PFAS or fluorinated substances, including bis-FASI compounds. Yet project-level permits can still omit PFAS monitoring when state agencies conclude that standards or approved methods do not support a permit condition. Sources: [S3], [S4], [S8], [S38], [S39], [S40].

The counterexample is just as important. BlueOval SK's DOE review shows what the relieved bottleneck looks like: water supply capacity, wastewater capacity, a dedicated treatment system, a long discharge pipeline, reuse routing, and contaminant containment were integrated into the project design. In those cases, water becomes a large capital and engineering package rather than a late surprise. Source: [S27].

The battery industry may be approaching the semiconductor industry's water moment. Semiconductor fabs now treat water as a core production utility, with industrial reclamation plants, brine reduction, reverse osmosis, ion exchange, and reuse designed into campus architecture. Battery factories do not all need the same ultrapure-water intensity, but upstream battery materials and recycling plants are starting to need the same mindset: water is not peripheral infrastructure; it is part of the process flow sheet. Sources: [S46], [S47], [S48], [S49].

The practical implication is that water permitting is becoming a selective manufacturing constraint. It will not stop every gigafactory. It will penalize projects whose discharge chemistry, ramp schedule, and local infrastructure do not match. It will favor projects that treat wastewater, brine, metals, solvents, and residuals as first-order manufacturing systems from site selection onward.
