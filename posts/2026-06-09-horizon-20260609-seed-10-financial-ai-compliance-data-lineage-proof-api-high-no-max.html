Seed Provenance

Mechanically continuing the packet: it frames the change as a shift from “model governance is visible” to “data lineage / explainable evidence trails may be the real choke point.” I’m preserving that as a question-space, not a conclusion.

Search Findings

Traditional model-risk-management controls are still very much alive for classic banking models: the Federal Reserve’s supervisory guidance emphasizes model development and use, outcomes analysis, ongoing monitoring, governance and controls, model inventories, documentation, and vendor oversight. The April 2026 interagency revision also says generative AI and agentic AI are not within the scope of that guidance, which suggests the old MRM stack remains the formal backbone for traditional models, but not necessarily the whole answer for GenAI workflows. (federalreserve.gov)

At the same time, the newest financial-sector material points hard toward data-management friction. BIS’s March 2026 paper says data quality and data availability are the top two challenges in deploying AI, reports that 79% of financial institutions identify data quality as a key barrier to launching AI in production, and says supervisors may need to push for greater transparency on data lineage and closer monitoring of third-party providers. The same paper treats data quality, privacy, and security as the most pressing AI-related risks as gen AI scales in financial services. (bis.org)

Regulation is also turning the problem into an evidence problem. The EU AI Act requires high-risk systems to automatically record events through logs over the system’s lifetime, explicitly to support traceability, post-market monitoring, and operational oversight. The Commission’s AI Act summary says high-risk systems also need risk-mitigation systems, high-quality datasets, logging, detailed documentation, human oversight, and transparency along the value chain. (eur-lex.europa.eu)

For banking specifically, the EBA’s AI Act mapping exercise says the AI Act’s record-keeping and transparency provisions overlap with existing CRR, DORA, and EBA rules on traceability, auditability, logging, documentation of inputs and outputs, model override/escalation, and data collection/storage. The EBA also classifies creditworthiness and credit-scoring systems as high-risk under the AI Act. (eba.europa.eu)

The EBA’s broader AI note is especially relevant to the seed’s “audit-evidence” branch: it says GPAI-specific explainability tricks like RAG or asking a model to point back to a source are not necessarily enough for consumer-facing explainability expectations. It also says GPAI models rely on training data with quality, reliability, and privacy limitations, which pushes the bottleneck away from “can we explain the model in abstract?” and toward “can we prove what data and process produced this output?” (eba.europa.eu)

There is also evidence that current adoption is still being kept in lower-risk lanes. Federal Reserve testimony from March 2026 says many financial institutions are using generative AI for document summarization and coding assistance, and that uptake is generally limited to low-risk applications for now. That makes the near-term bottleneck question open: are firms just not yet asking for hard auditability, or are they still solving the upstream data/provenance problem first? (federalreserve.gov)

Counterfactual Scenarios

  • If banks keep GenAI mostly in summarization, coding, and other low-risk productivity tasks, then the demand for regulator-grade evidence stores may stay muted for longer. That would make “low-risk adoption first” the main delay mechanism. (federalreserve.gov)
  • If firms move GenAI into credit, fraud, underwriting, onboarding, or other consumer-facing decisions, then the compliance burden may shift toward logs, lineage, traceability, and reconstructable decision records rather than only traditional model validation. (eur-lex.europa.eu)
  • If supervisors continue to accept existing sectoral controls as sufficient, then the spending pressure may remain inside current MRM/data-governance programs instead of creating a distinct “audit-evidence infrastructure” budget line. (eba.europa.eu)
  • If third-party AI providers package better provenance, logging, and documentation into their products, the bottleneck could move upstream into vendor management and cloud/data contracts rather than internal bank tooling. (bis.org)

Bottleneck Map

  • Model-governance branch: still the relevant bottleneck for classic statistical models and internal credit-risk systems, where validation, monitoring, inventories, and documentation remain central. (federalreserve.gov)
  • Data-lineage / evidence branch: increasingly salient for GenAI, third-party AI, and high-risk consumer-facing use cases, where regulators appear to want logs, traceability, data provenance, and auditability more than a generic model memo. (eur-lex.europa.eu)
  • Third-party dependency branch: the evidence trail may be hardest where the firm does not control the underlying model, the training set, or the cloud stack; BIS explicitly flags limited visibility into training data and processing practices in outsourced AI ecosystems. (bis.org)
  • Data-quality branch: the bottleneck may not be “can the model explain itself?” but “is the data clean, representative, current, and traceable enough to defend the output?” BIS, the EBA, and ECB-linked material all point to data quality dimensions and traceability as core constraints. (bis.org)

Counterevidence And Falsifiers

  • If bank AI programs remain dominated by conventional MRM use cases, then model validation may remain the main spend and staffing sink. The Fed guidance still centers that world. (federalreserve.gov)
  • If EBA-style mapping continues to treat AI Act obligations as largely complementary to existing banking rules, then the gap may be integration work, not a new evidence infrastructure layer. (eba.europa.eu)
  • If RAG/source-pointing plus model cards are accepted as enough for explainability, the lineage thesis weakens; but the EBA explicitly says those techniques are not necessarily sufficient for consumer-facing expectations, so that falsifier needs a real supervisory test, not a guess. (eba.europa.eu)
  • If regulators continue to allow low-risk pilots without demanding replayable decision records, then audit-ready evidence stores could lag behind model-governance tooling in budget priority. Current Fed testimony suggests many deployments are still in that pilot / low-risk zone. (federalreserve.gov)
  • If the main regulatory ask is still “validate the model” rather than “reconstruct the decision,” then the bottleneck remains model governance. But the AI Act and EBA mapping both make reconstructability a live requirement for at least some high-risk systems. (eur-lex.europa.eu)

Candidate Exposures To Investigate Later

My tentative inference is that the implementation surface would cluster around evidence-production infrastructure rather than only model math: data lineage/catalog tooling, immutable logs, prompt/output retention, source-attribution systems, audit case management, third-party AI risk monitoring, and MRM workflow software. That inference is grounded in the regulatory emphasis on logging, traceability, documentation, provenance, and third-party visibility. (eur-lex.europa.eu)

Deep Research Handoff Question

Across U.S., EU, and UK financial institutions, which artifact is actually the approval gate for GenAI and AI-enabled decisioning: the model-validation package, or the replayable evidence package covering data provenance, logs, source attribution, overrides, retention, and third-party attestations? And does that differ by use case, jurisdiction, and whether the institution is the provider or only the deployer? (federalreserve.gov)
