Cancer's Next Supply Chain Is a Radioisotope Clock

Novartis' two commercial RLT products reached $2.810B of 2025 net sales: Pluvicto at $1.994B and Lutathera at $816M. 200+ centers now order Pluvicto, ~130 more planned — after a 2023 manufacturing shortage.

By Bottleneck Briefs · Published 2026-06-23 · 12 min read

In brief

  • Novartis' two commercial RLT products reached $2.810B of 2025 net sales: Pluvicto at $1.994B and Lutathera at $816M.
  • 200+ centers now order Pluvicto, ~130 more planned — after a 2023 manufacturing shortage.
  • DOE's Ac-225 is still small-batch: ~50-100 mCi today, targeting 100-1,000 within ~4 years.
  • TerraPower's $450M Bellwether plant aims to lift global Ac-225 capacity twentyfold.
  • The scarce asset is qualified isotope supply, not the ligand: hot cells, DMFs, a half-life clock.

The oncology story usually starts with biology. A company finds a target, attaches a payload, runs a trial, and waits for regulators to judge whether the tumor response is strong enough. Radiopharmaceuticals fit that frame: a ligand brings a radioactive payload near a cancer cell, then the isotope delivers radiation over a short path.

That frame misses the thing that makes radiopharma different from most drugs. A radiopharmaceutical is a manufacturing event with a clock already running.

The product starts losing value before it reaches the patient. It needs isotope production, source material, hot-cell processing, radiochemical separation, cGMP manufacturing, quality release, a drug master file or letter of authorization, shielded transport, a licensed nuclear medicine team, and a patient appointment before decay turns inventory into waste. Ordinary drug supply chains can hide errors in inventory. Radiopharma cannot.

Pluvicto gave the market its first large commercial warning. Novartis won FDA approval in 2022 for eligible PSMA-positive metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer. In 2025, the FDA expanded the indication into an earlier use case for adults who had received androgen receptor pathway inhibitor therapy and were appropriate to delay taxane chemotherapy. Earlier use means a larger patient pool and more recurring demand.

Pluvicto also entered shortage in 2023. Novartis later said the FDA had classified the shortage as resolved, that supply was unconstrained, and that weekly production capacity had more than doubled since May 2023. The company also cited more than 200 active ordering centers and about 130 planned additions. The important lesson is that a clinically successful oncology product became a dose-release, site-approval, and delivery problem.

The commercial base is no longer hypothetical. Novartis' 2025 supplementary financial report shows Pluvicto net sales of $1.994 billion and Lutathera net sales of $816 million, or $2.810 billion combined. Reuters' rounded $2.8 billion figure is therefore backed by company financials, not just market commentary. The same Reuters piece says radioligand therapy accounts for nearly 40% of Novartis' cancer R&D investments, but the primary transcript or slide behind that percentage was not found in the targeted pass. That distinction matters: product sales are primary-verified; the R&D-share figure remains a Reuters-reported executive statement.

Novartis responded with geography. Indianapolis received FDA approval in January 2024, adding a 70,000 square-foot radioligand therapy site and raising RLT production capacity to 250,000 doses in 2024 and beyond. Carlsbad added a 10,000 square-foot western U.S. site. Denton, Texas adds a 46,000 square-foot facility expected to become operational in 2028. The facility map shows the operating model: production has to move closer to patient regions because time is part of the product.

The upstream constraint is tighter for actinium-225. DOE describes Ac-225 as a high-demand isotope with limited availability. Historical supply comes from thorium-229 decay, while accelerator-produced supply runs through the Tri-Lab system involving Brookhaven, Los Alamos, and Oak Ridge. The report's captured DOE data show more than 1,102 millicuries distributed through the accelerator route, with current batch size around 50-100 millicuries and a plan to reach 100-1,000 millicuries per batch over about four years.

This is not a generic commodity shortage. The useful object is qualified isotope. Brookhaven's All-Purpose Hot Cells page says available hot-cell space had limited Ac-225 processing. An $8.5 million refurbishment brought dormant hot cells back online, doubled DOE processing sites for Ac-225, and allowed Brookhaven to process irradiated thorium targets and ship product to customers. Oak Ridge adds the regulatory control point: FDA acknowledged ORNL's Drug Master File for Ac-225 nitrate derived from thorium-229, allowing companies to reference the filing in submissions.

Private capital has moved directly into the choke point. TerraPower Isotopes broke ground in Philadelphia on Bellwether Laboratory, a 250,000 square-foot, $450 million cGMP Ac-225 facility. Pennsylvania's economic development office said TerraPower evaluated more than 350 U.S. sites, made 49 visits across eight metro areas, and expects 225 full-time jobs plus 500 construction jobs.

PanTera is building another route in Belgium. The IBA and SCK CEN venture says it obtained nuclear and environmental permits, broke ground on its Actinium Production Center, and targets first commercial Ac-225 supply in 2029. Nusano opened a 190,000 square-foot West Valley City, Utah facility designed to produce more than 40 radioisotopes. ITM opened a Lu-177 facility near Munich that it calls the world's largest and says will increase capacity tenfold when fully operational.

The contract layer says drug developers understand the same bottleneck. Niowave signed a long-term Ac-225 agreement with Novartis. Bayer and NorthStar signed an Ac-225 supply agreement. Nucleus RadioPharma and BWXT signed an agreement around GMP-grade Ac-225 and I-123, with BWXT maintaining drug master files and letters of authorization. Ratio Therapeutics signed supply agreements with TerraPower and Nusano. Ariceum signed with Nusano and ITM. These contracts reserve position in a regulated isotope clock before patient demand fully arrives.

The clinical pull on Ac-225 is also getting more concrete. The targeted pass located the ASCO/JCO abstract identity for Novartis' AcTION Phase 1 study of [225Ac]Ac-PSMA-617, while Reuters and Urology Times report PSA50 responses of 52.5% in patients with prior Lu-177 PSMA radioligand exposure and 56.3% at the 10 MBq dose. Novartis' own ASCO release says the data support further development and lists two Phase 3 trials, PSMAcTION and AcTFirst. The careful read is not that the Ac-225 market is proven, but that the option value of reliable Ac-225 supply has moved from science project toward late-stage pipeline infrastructure.

Where the value can shift

The economics can sit in different places as the market matures. Clinical developers own targets, ligands, trials, brands, and physician demand. Isotope producers own source material, irradiation routes, hot cells, cGMP isotope API, DMFs, and capacity reservations. CDMOs and radiopharma manufacturers own the conversion layer between isotope and usable dose. Hospitals own nuclear medicine teams, radiation-safety workflows, and patient calendars. Logistics providers own shielded transport and delivery timing.

That value split explains why large drug companies bought radiopharma platforms while suppliers built facilities. Lilly agreed to buy POINT Biopharma for about $1.4 billion. AstraZeneca bought Fusion in a deal valued up to about $2.4 billion. Bristol Myers Squibb acquired RayzeBio for about $4.1 billion. Novartis bought Mariana Oncology for $1 billion upfront plus milestones. Those deals were about drug pipelines, but the operating risk sits in the industrial stack behind the pipeline.

Public-company exposure should be read as exposure, not a recommendation. Novartis has the clearest commercial network through Pluvicto, Lutathera, Indianapolis, Carlsbad, Denton, and later U.S. site expansion. Lilly, AstraZeneca, and Bristol Myers Squibb have pipeline exposure through POINT, Fusion, and RayzeBio. Bayer appears in the supply-contract map through NorthStar. BWXT appears as a supplier and DMF/LOA node through the Nucleus agreement and its NorthStar relationship. The report also points to private or government-linked nodes such as TerraPower Isotopes, PanTera, Nusano, SHINE, ITM, DOE, BNL, and ORNL.

RayzeBio shows how quickly a pipeline acquisition becomes a manufacturing question. BMS announced the acquisition in December 2023 while RayzeBio was completing construction of an Indianapolis in-house facility and expected GMP drug production to begin in the first half of 2024. Later BMS and RayzeBio materials describe a 77,000 square-foot hub, fully operational for clinical orders by July 2025, with more than $160 million invested and human clinical doses already being produced. The exact first GMP production date was not public, but the direction is clear enough for the operating model: large pharma does not only buy radiopharma molecules; it buys the factory calendar, release process, and isotope-to-dose logistics behind them.

How tight is the constraint?

Actinium-225 remains the tightest part of the system. DOE's route is moving from small research and clinical supply toward larger batches, but the plan still depends on specialized accelerators, hot cells, operators, and radiochemical processing. TerraPower, PanTera, Nusano, BWXT/NorthStar, Niowave, and DOE can relieve the market only after facilities validate output, customer contracts convert into deliveries, and regulatory documentation proves reusable across clinical programs.

Lutetium-177 looks less scarce than Ac-225 because commercial products already use it and multiple suppliers are scaling. ITM and SHINE both address that layer, and Novartis has its own RLT network. Lu-177 can still become tight if earlier-line indications and new launches outrun production, source-material recycling, fill-finish, and treatment-center onboarding.

The next bottleneck may move downstream. If isotope supply catches up, regional drug-product manufacturing, quality release, shielded logistics, hospital nuclear medicine staff, radiation-safety workflows, and patient scheduling can become the constraint. Novartis' treatment-center expansion after Pluvicto is the clue. In this market, solving one node moves the clock to the next node.

The base rate

Medical isotope shortages have happened before. The Mo-99/Tc-99m crisis in 2009-2010 came from outages at key research reactors and forced policy work around economic sustainability and outage reserve capacity. The IAEA described a 30% supply shortfall after reactor outages in 2008-2009, with postponed diagnostic procedures. A 2024 Petten Mo-99 outage again led to appointment cancellations.

Radiopharma differs from diagnostic isotope generators because the therapeutic supply chain adds cGMP isotope API, drug conjugation, commercial launches, companion imaging, and oncology treatment slots. The historical base rate still matters: medical isotope systems can stay fragile for years when a small number of production nodes serve a large clinical market.

The strongest counterargument is supply response. Pluvicto's shortage was resolved. Novartis is expanding. TerraPower, PanTera, Nusano, ITM, SHINE, BWXT/NorthStar, Niowave, DOE, ORNL, and BNL all point toward more capacity. Clinical demand may also disappoint. Reuters reported early Novartis actinium-drug data with activity, but the captured report also noted hematologic toxicity and serious adverse events. If clinical uptake grows slower than capacity, isotope scarcity fades as the main constraint.

What to watch

  • Ac-225 supply agreements that name GMP grade, delivery timing, DMF access, or letters of authorization.
  • TerraPower Bellwether construction, validation, customer contracts, and the timing behind the twentyfold capacity claim.
  • PanTera's 2029 commercial supply target and whether permits translate into qualified output.
  • DOE batch-size progress from 50-100 mCi toward 100-1,000 mCi, plus BNL and ORNL processing redundancy.
  • Novartis' Denton 2028 site, Winter Park 2029 plan, active treatment-center count, and time from diagnosis to dose.
  • Lu-177 scale-up at ITM, SHINE, Novartis, Eckert & Ziegler, Isotopia, and other suppliers.
  • Hospital nuclear medicine capacity, radiation-safety staffing, patient scheduling, and shielded logistics.
  • New M&A or supply agreements that tie pharma pipelines to named isotope, CDMO, or treatment-center nodes.

Cancer therapy will still be judged by clinical data. Radiopharma adds another test. The company with the best drug also needs a clock it can control.
