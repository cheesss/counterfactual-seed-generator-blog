AMR Wastewater Surveillance Becomes A qPCR/Metagenomics Standardization Bottleneck

Wastewater AMR surveillance is entering a transition. The first phase asked whether sewage could reveal antibiotic resistance signals at population scale. The answer is now clearly yes.

By Bottleneck Briefs · Published 2026-06-29 · 4 min read

Wastewater AMR surveillance is entering a transition. The first phase asked whether sewage could reveal antibiotic resistance signals at population scale. The answer is now clearly yes. The second phase asks whether those signals can be made comparable enough to guide public-health decisions across laboratories, cities, countries, and time. That answer is still incomplete.

The bottleneck is no longer simply whether wastewater can be sampled or whether sequencing machines exist. The hard problem is standardization.

WHO's 2025 summary on wastewater and environmental surveillance for AMR says the approach is technically and operationally feasible, but not yet routine. It also says there are no standardized AMR WES methods and that sampling, transport, lab methods, and bioinformatics workflows can materially change observed AMR and ARG profiles. Source: WHO, URL: https://cdn.who.int/media/docs/default-source/wash-documents/wash-related-diseases/wes-summary-for-amr---final-1-dec.pdf?download=true&amp;sfvrsn=fb4aadba_3

That distinction matters. A wastewater plant can collect a composite sample. A lab can extract DNA. A qPCR or ddPCR instrument can quantify selected ARGs. A sequencer can generate metagenomic reads. But public-health surveillance needs more than detection. It needs agreed targets, validated assays, common controls, reference materials, consistent units, metadata, pipeline reproducibility, quality thresholds, and a governance process that says what action follows from a signal.

Europe is now forcing that issue. The 2024 recast Urban Wastewater Treatment Directive requires AMR monitoring in urban wastewater for agglomerations of 100,000 population equivalent and above, and requires implementing acts by 2 July 2026 to define minimum frequency and harmonized methodology. Source: EUR-Lex, URL: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2024/3019/oj/eng

The EEA/Eionet pilot shows what harmonization looks like in practice: qPCR and culture methods, common DNA reference material, negative controls, standard reporting templates, quality assurance, comparable indicators, and central reporting. It also shows why this is difficult: countries differ in capability, funding, methods, and objectives. Source: EEA, URL: https://www.eea.europa.eu/en/analysis/publications/antimicrobial-resistance-in-european-surface-waters-a-developing-area

The U.S. experience shows the infrastructure side is no longer blank slate. CDC's wastewater program scaled rapidly during COVID-19, and CDC links pre-pandemic AMR wastewater work to later NWSS best-practice development. That infrastructure can be extended, but AMR is not SARS-CoV-2. AMR is multi-target, multi-host, environmentally distributed, and clinically ambiguous unless the measurement system is designed to connect genes, organisms, resistance phenotypes, and health outcomes. Source: CDC, URL: https://www.cdc.gov/advanced-molecular-detection/php/success-stories/wastewater-surveillance.html ; CDC, URL: https://www.cdc.gov/antimicrobial-resistance/stories/wastewater-testing.html

qPCR and ddPCR are likely to be the near-term backbone for standardized ARG monitoring because they are targeted, sensitive, and operationally practical. Yet their usefulness depends on target selection, primer/probe validation, controls, inhibition checks, reporting units, and inter-lab calibration. Metagenomics is broader and better for discovery, but it depends heavily on sequencing depth, DNA extraction, database choice, mapping thresholds, normalization, and pipeline versioning.

That is the investment and policy implication without turning it into a trade recommendation: the scarce asset is not just sequencing capacity. The scarce asset is decision-grade comparability.

In brief

  • The thesis is mostly supported: in established wastewater-surveillance systems, AMR surveillance appears more constrained by standardization, comparability, reference materials, bioinformatics, and governance than by the basic ability to collect samples or generate sequence data.
  • The thesis is not universal: in non-sewered, decentralized, underfunded, or low-capacity settings, sampling access, laboratory consumables, trained staff, and sequencing cost can still be binding.
  • qPCR and ddPCR are strong candidates for routine standardized ARG panels, but they require validated assays, common controls, agreed targets, common units, and inter-lab ring trials.
  • Metagenomics is valuable for discovery and broader resistome characterization, but it increases the burden on bioinformatics standardization, database governance, sequencing depth, and normalization.
  • WHO, EU, EEA, ECDC, CDC, NIST, ISO-adjacent work, and peer-reviewed inter-lab studies all point toward the same unresolved layer: turning detection into comparable public-health evidence.

Key figures

  • CDC described NWSS as launched in September 2020 and scaled by July 2022 to 46 states, five cities, two territories, more than 1,200 testing sites, and more than 130 million people. Source: CDC, URL: https://www.cdc.gov/advanced-molecular-detection/php/success-stories/wastewater-surveillance.html
  • CDC states that nearly 80 percent of U.S. households are served by sewer systems, which supports scale but also highlights exclusion of non-sewered households. Source: CDC, URL: https://www.cdc.gov/advanced-molecular-detection/php/success-stories/wastewater-surveillance.html
  • EU Directive 2024/3019 requires AMR monitoring in urban wastewater for agglomerations of 100,000 population equivalent and above and requires a harmonized methodology implementing act by 2 July 2026. Source: EUR-Lex, URL: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2024/3019/oj/eng
  • EEA describes an Eionet working group involving experts from 14 European countries and pilot sampling across ten European countries between April and December 2024. Source: EEA, URL: https://www.eea.europa.eu/en/analysis/publications/antimicrobial-resistance-in-european-surface-waters-a-developing-area
  • An EU-WISH-linked Eurosurveillance article reported that, among 27 surveyed European countries, 11 had operative AMR wastewater-based surveillance. Full article parsing was not available in the current browsing session; source metadata and snippet were accessible. Source: Eurosurveillance, URL: https://www.eurosurveillance.org/content/10.2807/1560-7917.ES.2026.31.3.2500289
  • A qPCR inter-lab calibration study reported inter-laboratory variation below 28 percent for quantifiable ARG targets under common DNA extract pools and identical protocols. Source: Rocha et al., URL: https://publikationen.bibliothek.kit.edu/1000087205/149184870
  • A qPCR standardization framework reviewed 117 peer-reviewed studies and proposed five core monitoring targets: sul1, tetA, intI1, vanA, and blaCTX-M. Source: Keenum et al., URL: https://agris.fao.org/search/en/providers/122535/records/65df43ce63b8185d9cabcb97
  • A U.S. ddPCR study quantified ARGs in wastewater solids from 163 wastewater treatment plants across 40 states. Source: Nature Communications, URL: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-71195-4
