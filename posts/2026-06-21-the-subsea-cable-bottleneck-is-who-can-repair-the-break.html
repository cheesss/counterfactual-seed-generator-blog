The Subsea Cable Bottleneck Is Who Can Repair the Break

Submarine cables carry over 99% of international data; the risk is repair. The scarce asset is a ready cable ship, not more fiber. Two-thirds of repair ships reach end-of-life by 2040. Sustaining the fleet needs about $3 billion for roughly 20 ships.

Global connectivity depends on an industrial service most people never see: the ability to find a damaged cable, dispatch the right ship, secure permission to enter the right water, recover the cable from the seabed, splice it, test it, and put it back into service.

That repair chain used to look like marine maintenance. It now looks like strategic infrastructure.

The reason is simple. ITU says submarine cables carry more than 99% of international data exchange and reported more than 200 cable repairs worldwide in 2023. ICPC describes a network of about 500 cable systems and roughly 1.8 million kilometers of infrastructure, with about 150 to 200 telecom cable faults per year. Most faults come from anchors and fishing rather than sabotage.

That base rate argues against panic. The cable system fails often and gets repaired often. The sharper question is different: what happens when faults cluster in politically sensitive waters, conflict zones, or shallow chokepoints where repair access, insurance, permits, and trusted suppliers matter as much as the cable itself?

The scarce asset is readiness

TeleGeography and Infra-Analytics put a hard number on the maintenance problem. Their report summary projects a 48% net increase in deployed cable kilometers by 2040, while roughly two-thirds of cable maintenance ships reach end-of-service life by then. They estimate that about $3 billion would fund 15 replacement ships and five additional ships to sustain current service levels and avoid repair delays.

That forecast changes the bottleneck. Capacity is not just fiber pairs, landing stations, and repeaters. Capacity also means ships, crews, spares, depots, ROVs, ploughs, marine survey capability, permits, insurance, and a maintenance agreement that gives a cable owner priority when several systems need attention.

The timing is the uncomfortable part. The same analysis finds that roughly two-thirds of today’s maintenance ships reach end-of-service life by 2040, just as deployed cable kilometers rise by nearly half. A fleet that is simultaneously shrinking through retirement and being asked to cover more route-kilometers is the textbook setup for lengthening repair queues — not because any single break is hard, but because the right ships are already booked. A maintenance agreement matters precisely here: it converts ship availability into a contracted priority, so a member’s fault is attended before a non-member’s. The scarce, ownable asset is therefore not a cable but a claim on a ship’s time.

SubCom says it operates eight purpose-built cable ships and fifteen submersible tools. NEC says it has laid more than 450,000 kilometers of submarine cables and offers design, supply, installation, testing, and maintenance. Nokia's ASN transaction matters because France moved to take control of a leading submarine network business with deployed cable, marine operations, and a fleet capability attached.

This is where sovereignty enters. A state that treats cable repair as a private telecom service will depend on whichever ships, insurers, crews, and permits remain available in a crisis. A state that treats repair as strategic infrastructure will care about domestic or trusted vessel access, supplier control, landing rules, spares, and naval or coast-guard coordination.

The policy system has noticed

The European Union has moved from concern to operational planning. The Commission's cable-security work covers prevention, detection, response, recovery, and deterrence. In 2025, EU work on mapping and coordinated risk assessment covered capacity, ownership, technical specifications, landing sites, incidents, maintenance, repair processes, physical and cyber threats, supply-chain dependencies, legal factors, and administrative factors.

Reuters reported that the Commission proposed boosting surveillance and creating a fleet of vessels available for emergency undersea cable repairs. That idea moves cable repair out of the background. The policy object becomes a reserve or trusted repair capacity, not only a better map of cables.

The United States is hardening the licensing side. The FCC's 2025 submarine cable landing rule requires more detailed system information and adds foreign-adversary, covered-equipment, cybersecurity, and physical-security controls. A cable landing license now sits closer to national security review than routine telecom paperwork.

The cable owner needs more than redundancy

Hyperscalers understand the demand side. Meta's Project Waterworth is a multi-billion-dollar, multi-year subsea system spanning more than 50,000 kilometers with 24 fiber pairs, and Meta explicitly links the project to AI-era connectivity. It also says the design will use deep-water routing and enhanced burial in high-risk shallow areas.

Google's Firmina shows a different resilience design. Google says Firmina was designed to run from a single power source at one end if other power sources become unavailable. That is a clever way to design around certain failure modes. It does not replace the need to repair physical infrastructure when a cable breaks.

The repair problem becomes more acute in contested waters. Reuters' Hormuz report makes the point directly: physical cable repair may be technically routine, but conflict-zone decisions can depend on vessel-owner risk tolerance, insurers, mines, and permits to enter territorial waters. Satellites cannot replace subsea cables at comparable cost and volume.

That is the narrow bottleneck. A cable can have redundancy on paper and still face a repair delay if the right ship cannot enter the area, if war-risk insurance fails, if a permit stalls, if a supplier lacks trust clearance, or if multiple incidents compete for the same marine assets.

Maintenance agreements become strategic contracts

The Atlantic Cable Maintenance Agreement gives a glimpse of the institutional layer. ACMA describes itself as a cooperative maintenance agreement with more than 60 members and one of the largest dedicated maintenance fleets in the business, with three cable ships positioned for its region. That kind of agreement converts ship availability into a shared resilience product.

The same logic applies to cable depots, spare repeaters, joint kits, route surveys, cable burial, marine domain awareness, and trusted suppliers. The owner of the cable wants bandwidth. The operator of a national economy wants repair assurance.

Who controls the constraint

The repair base is a small, specialized world, and that concentration is the point. Cooperative maintenance agreements pool and reserve vessels: ACMA has more than 60 members but only three dedicated cable ships for its region, and a handful of operators do most of the actual work. Global Marine has performed roughly a third of all fiber-optic cable maintenance operations globally; Orange Marine and Elettra’s six ships have laid more than 220,000 kilometers and carried out nearly 550 repairs. Several of these shares and repair counts are operator-reported; basin-by-basin vessel utilization and day-rate data are not public, so current scarcity is inferred, not measured. The vertically integrated system suppliers capture the lifecycle economics — SubCom with eight cable ships and fifteen submersible tools, NEC with 450,000-plus kilometers laid and a Japan-made supply chain, and Alcatel Submarine Networks with more than 750,000 kilometers deployed and now under French-State control. Above the commercial layer, sovereignty is closing in: the FCC’s 2025 landing-license rule adds foreign-adversary and covered-equipment controls, the EU is planning an emergency repair fleet and a cable-security toolbox, and the US keeps two US-flagged cable ships on 24-hour notice. So the real control points are not just ships but approved ships, approved crews, approved equipment, landing-license conditions, war-risk insurance, and permission to enter territorial waters. The party that holds repair assurance, not the party that owns the fiber, decides how fast a break heals.

The counterargument matters

The system is not collapsing. ICPC's data cut against the most alarmist version of the story: cable length has expanded while reported fault counts have remained broadly within the 150 to 200 annual range. Better burial, routing, installation, and operational practice appear to have reduced fault rates per kilometer. Most incidents still come from anchors and fishing, not hostile action.

There is even a way the policy response backfires. The FCC is tightening landing-license review and trusted-equipment rules at the same time it tries to streamline deployment; if compliance, route-data disclosure, and foreign-adversary due diligence grow heavy enough, the practical result could be fewer cables on fewer routes — more concentration, not less, which is the opposite of resilience. The constructive version points the other way. The EU pairs its emergency-fleet idea with maritime domain awareness — AIS-anomaly detection, seabed monitoring, and patrol integration around landing sites and shallow routes — so that prevention and faster detection reduce how often the scarce repair ships have to sail at all. Either way the variable to watch is the same: not how many cables are announced, but how much trusted, ready repair capacity sits behind them.

That makes the bottleneck conditional. It is not a claim that cables are breaking everywhere or that sabotage explains most damage. It is a claim that repair capacity becomes strategic when more traffic concentrates in higher-capacity systems, more projects serve AI and cloud demand, and more faults occur near waters where permission, insurance, and sovereign trust slow the response.

The danger lives in the tails, not the average. The same UK national-security committee that found no imminent connectivity threat also warned that traffic is concentrating into fewer, higher-capacity cables, which turns each one into a higher-value target — and the TeleGeography forecast shows the maintenance fleet aging into exactly that concentration. The thesis bites when faults stop being isolated and accidental. In the 2025 Taiwan incident, weather kept the coast guard from boarding a suspect vessel and a repair was expected only weeks later; Baltic and Hormuz cases show how attribution delay, permits, mines, insurers, and vessel-owner risk can stall a technically routine splice. A 2022 US analysis counted only two US-flagged cable ships on 24-hour notice and warned that peacetime maintenance agreements can fail under wartime force majeure, flag, crew, and insurance constraints. Redundancy on paper does not help if every nearby ship is committed, uninsurable, or barred from the water. That is the precise condition under which a repair queue, not a fiber shortage, becomes the thing that decides whether a country stays connected.

The question to watch is not only how many new cables are announced. Watch cable-ship orders, emergency fleet plans, long-term maintenance agreements, landing-license conditions, state ownership of suppliers, depot buildouts, spare-kit inventories, route-hardening language, and war-risk insurance treatment.

Bandwidth gets the attention because it is easy to sell. Repair readiness is harder to see. In a stressed network, the decisive asset may be the right ship, in the right jurisdiction, with the right crew, permission, insurance, and spare parts before the outage becomes a strategic event.
