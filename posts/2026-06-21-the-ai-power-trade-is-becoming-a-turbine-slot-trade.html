The AI Power Trade Is Becoming a Turbine Slot Trade

The standard story about AI power is easy to summarize: data centers are growing faster than the grid. That is true, but it is already becoming too broad to be useful. A hyperscaler can sign a power purchase agreement. A utility can announce new generation. A developer can point to a gas pipeline and a large parcel of land. None of that produces firm megawatts on a useful timeline if the critical machine in the middle of the project already has a five-year queue.

The narrower bottleneck is dated access to power equipment: large-frame gas turbines, aeroderivative turbines, reciprocating gas engines, EPC crews, hot-section parts, control systems, and long-term service agreements. In the last power cycle, a developer usually advanced the site, permits, grid studies, fuel plan, and financing before the turbine slot became the center of the project. The AI cycle is flipping that order. The delivery slot itself is becoming a strategic asset.

That is why "more power" is not the right unit of analysis. The better question is: who can prove that a specific machine, construction crew, fuel interface, and service package will be available in a specific year?

The demand side explains why this narrow market suddenly matters. The U.S. Department of Energy's Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory estimated that U.S. data-center electricity use rose from 58 TWh in 2014 to about 176 TWh in 2023, with a possible range of 325 to 580 TWh by 2028 (LBNL report). The IEA's Energy and AI work puts global data-center demand on a path from roughly 415 TWh in 2024 to about 945 TWh by 2030 in its base case (IEA). Those forecasts do not decide which fuel wins. They explain why speed, firmness, and deliverability have become more valuable than elegant planning diagrams.

The supply side is where the story gets more interesting. GE Vernova said its Gas Power equipment backlog and slot reservation agreements rose from 83 GW to 100 GW in the first quarter of 2026, with 21 GW of new gas equipment contracts and 19 GW of slot reservation agreements in that quarter alone (GE Vernova). That language sounds financial because the bottleneck has become financialized. Developers are not only buying turbines. They are buying a place in line.

Reuters and Wood Mackenzie point to the same pressure from a different angle. Reuters reported that planned and under-development U.S. gas-fired capacity more than tripled in 2025 to roughly 252 GW, while combined-cycle capital costs more than doubled over 12 to 24 months to at least $2,400 per kW (Reuters). Wood Mackenzie separately said gas turbine prices could reach about $600 per kW by the end of 2027, a 195% increase from 2019 (Wood Mackenzie). Price is only one signal. Lead time is the more revealing one. When a turbine quote moves from equipment procurement into calendar scarcity, the whole development stack changes.

This is why the substitution layer matters. Developers are not waiting patiently for the perfect H-class combined-cycle plant. Wartsila has announced multiple U.S. data-center power orders using gas engines, including projects in Ohio and Texas. Cummins signed a multi-year agreement with Circe Energy to supply natural-gas generator sets for behind-the-meter prime-power microgrids serving high-performance-computing data centers in West Texas (Cummins). These are not perfect substitutes for central-station generation. They are evidence that time has become part of the product.

The named-project layer makes the bottleneck easier to see. NRG's partnership with GE Vernova and Kiewit is organized around 5.4 GW of new combined-cycle capacity in ERCOT and PJM, with reserved capacity expected online in 2029 and 2030. Chevron, Engine No. 1, and GE Vernova announced a plan to use seven GE 7HA turbines for up to 4 GW of power for U.S. data centers (Chevron). Tallgrass and Mitsubishi Power announced turbine allocation for the Cheyenne Power Hub in Wyoming. These are not generic power headlines. They are claims on equipment, EPC capacity, fuel access, and future service labor.

Markets can miss this because megawatts look fungible from far away. A chart can put solar, batteries, gas, interconnection queues, and data-center demand on the same axis. A project manager sees a less liquid market: a turbine model, a delivery year, a down payment, a transformer interface, a contractor, a maintenance interval, and a service contract. The economic rent moves toward the narrowest dated component in that chain.

There is a serious counterargument. This may be a late-cycle equipment squeeze rather than a durable structural constraint. AI load forecasts could prove too high. Some announced campuses may be delayed or canceled. OEMs are expanding capacity. GE Vernova is investing in Greenville. Siemens Energy and Mitsubishi Heavy Industries are working through their own capacity programs. Wartsila is expanding technical production capacity in Vaasa. If the demand curve bends down or the manufacturing response arrives quickly enough, today's reservation premium can turn into tomorrow's over-ordering problem.

That is the right caution. The point is not that gas turbines are the only AI power constraint, or that every announced data-center power project will be built. The point is narrower: once developers treat a delivery slot as scarce property, the center of gravity moves upstream from the power plant owner to the companies that can guarantee equipment, construction, and service availability on a dated schedule.

What To Watch

  • Slot reservation agreements and the rate at which they convert into firm turbine orders.
  • Quoted delivery years for large-frame turbines, aeroderivatives, and gas-engine packages.
  • EPC awards that bundle OEM equipment, construction, and service capacity.
  • Hot-gas-path parts, field labor, and long-term service backlog disclosures.
  • Substitution into gas engines, private grids, batteries, and other faster-to-build architectures.
  • Project cancellations or delays that would show the reservation queue was overbuilt.

The AI power trade is usually framed as a contest for electrons. The more precise version may be a contest for dated industrial capability. In this cycle, the strategic asset is not just a future power plant. It is the right machine, with the right crew and service package, arriving before the demand window closes.
