Working bottleneck question: as financial firms adopt AI, does the binding compliance constraint sit in model governance/model validation, or in reconstructable data lineage, prompt/output logs, source attribution, approval trails, and audit-ready evidence stores?

First-pass bias: the evidence does not cleanly separate “model governance” from “data-lineage proof.” Regulators often speak in model-risk/governance language, but the operational proof burden increasingly appears to require data provenance, logging, traceability, third-party oversight, and reconstructable decision evidence.

Search Findings

Financial AI adoption is no longer hypothetical. The 2024 Bank of England/FCA survey found that 75% of surveyed UK financial firms were already using AI and another 10% planned to use it within three years; foundation models accounted for 17% of reported AI use cases. However, the adoption pattern remains cautious: 55% of use cases had some degree of automated decision-making, but only 2% were fully autonomous, and 62% of all AI use cases were rated low materiality by firms. 영국은행 +1

The data-lineage thesis has direct support from the same survey. Four of the five largest perceived current AI risks were data-related: data privacy/protection, data quality, data security, and data bias/representativeness. Respondents also identified data protection and privacy as the largest regulatory constraint to AI use. This points toward “can we prove the data was appropriate, controlled, and traceable?” as a possible bottleneck, not merely “can we validate the model?” 영국은행 +1

Third-party AI use may convert model governance into evidence-infrastructure demand. One-third of current AI use cases in the UK survey were third-party implementations, up from 17% in 2022; risk/compliance had a high share of third-party implementations, and the largest providers accounted for a large share of cloud, model, and data provider mentions. Firms also reported only partial understanding of AI technologies more often than complete understanding, largely because of third-party models. 영국은행 +1

Model governance is still a live bottleneck. The BoE/FCA survey found that common AI governance components include an accountable person, an AI framework/principles/guidelines, and data governance. FINRA’s 2024 notice also frames GenAI under existing technology-neutral obligations and says firms using GenAI in supervisory systems should address technology governance, including model risk management, data privacy and integrity, reliability, and accuracy. 영국은행 +1

US regulatory posture currently looks more “existing obligations applied to AI” than “new standalone AI-lineage law.” FINRA explicitly stated that its GenAI notice creates no new legal or regulatory requirements. The FCA similarly says it does not plan extra AI regulations and will rely on existing frameworks. This is counterevidence against a near-term, regulation-mandated external “AI audit evidence store” boom in all jurisdictions. FINRA +1

The OCC’s 2026 revised model-risk guidance reinforces the model-governance side: it covers model development/use, model validation and monitoring, governance and controls, and third-party/vendor products. However, it also states that generative AI and agentic AI models are not within the scope of that guidance, which leaves a gap: existing MRM remains central, but GenAI/agentic systems may need additional control/evidence architecture outside classic MRM. OCC.gov

The EU AI Act creates stronger support for the audit-evidence thesis in high-risk AI contexts. Article 10 requires data governance practices covering data collection processes, origin of data, and original collection purpose for personal data; Article 12 requires automatic logging for high-risk AI systems to support traceability and monitoring; Article 19 says financial institutions must maintain logs as part of internal governance documentation; Article 26 similarly requires deployers to manage input data, monitor operation, keep logs, and maintain those logs under financial-services documentation rules. Legacy bank data architecture is already a known friction point. The Basel Committee’s 2026 BCBS 239 newsletter states that data lineage—traceability from origin to final use—remains challenging because of legacy systems, distributed data estates, dynamic lineage, vendor-solution difficulty, and the resource-intensive nature of maintaining lineage. It also notes that AI output quality depends on high-quality data, making robust data management more important as AI adoption develops. 국제 결제 은행

SEC signals are mixed but relevant. The SEC’s 2023 predictive-data-analytics proposal would require broker-dealers and investment advisers to address conflicts of interest from certain investor-facing technologies and keep written policies/procedures plus books and records if adopted. Its 2025 examination priorities include AI and emerging technologies alongside controls intended to protect investor information, records, and assets. SEC AI-washing enforcement also shows that firms’ claims about AI usage may require evidence that the stated AI capabilities actually exist. SEC +2 SEC +2

UK Parliament’s 2026 Treasury Committee report is useful counterweight. It says the UK has no AI-specific financial regulation and regulators rely on existing frameworks, but also identifies transparency gaps in credit/insurance AI decisions, uncertainty around accountability, and concerns over dependence on major AI/cloud providers. It recommends clearer guidance, AI-specific stress testing, and designation of major AI/cloud providers under the Critical Third Parties regime. 영국 의회 +1

Counterfactual Scenarios

MRM absorption scenario Financial AI compliance remains mostly an extension of model-risk management, supervision, vendor governance, and existing recordkeeping. Data lineage is required, but it is embedded inside MRM/GRC workflows rather than becoming a distinct infrastructure category. This scenario gains support from FINRA’s technology-neutral posture, the FCA’s reliance on existing frameworks, and OCC’s continued emphasis on model-risk governance. FINRA +2 FCA +2

Audit-evidence infrastructure scenario High-materiality AI use cases force firms to maintain reconstructable decision trails: input data origin, model version, retrieval sources, prompt/output logs, human reviewer, approval status, and downstream action. This scenario is most plausible where EU AI Act high-risk obligations, automated decision-making, credit/insurance transparency, or regulator/examiner evidence requests converge. Low-risk productivity containment scenario Firms keep AI mainly in low-materiality productivity, summarization, internal process optimization, customer support, and compliance-assist use cases. In that world, full audit-evidence infrastructure develops slowly because high-stakes, autonomous decisioning remains limited. The BoE/FCA evidence that only 2% of use cases are fully autonomous and 62% are low materiality supports this scenario as a real falsifier. 영국은행 +1

Third-party opacity scenario The bottleneck becomes less about internal model validation and more about proving control over external models, cloud platforms, data vendors, embedded AI tools, and concentration risk. The BoE/FCA survey’s third-party concentration findings and the UK Treasury Committee’s concern over reliance on major US technology firms support this scenario. 영국은행 +1

Privacy-limited logging scenario Firms need audit logs, but data protection and privacy constraints limit what can be retained. This could make the bottleneck not “more logs” but “privacy-preserving evidence”: redaction, tokenization, access controls, retention policies, and defensible deletion. Data protection/privacy being the largest regulatory constraint makes this a central branch. 영국은행

Counterevidence And Falsifiers

The strongest counterevidence is that several major regulators are not creating AI-specific financial rules. FINRA says its GenAI notice does not create new requirements, and the FCA says it does not plan extra AI regulation. That suggests AI compliance may remain a governance/process extension, not a new standalone data-lineage proof market. FINRA +1

The second counterevidence is adoption materiality. If most AI use cases remain low materiality and only a small fraction are fully autonomous, then audit-grade decision reconstruction may be necessary only for narrow use cases such as credit, insurance, compliance surveillance, fraud, and regulated advice. 영국은행 +1

The third counterevidence is that banks already have BCBS 239-style data governance and MRM infrastructure. If AI lineage can be absorbed into existing enterprise data governance, model inventory, SIEM/logging, and GRC systems, external category formation may be slower and more fragmented. 국제 결제 은행 +1

The fourth counterevidence is privacy. The same evidence that supports audit logs also makes unrestricted logging difficult. If data protection, retention minimization, and confidential information risks dominate, firms may avoid storing full prompts/outputs and instead keep thinner metadata trails. 영국은행 +1

Concrete falsifiers to collect in Deep Research: bank RFP language; vendor revenue segmented by AI governance versus data lineage versus GRC; enforcement cases requiring reconstruction of AI decisions; examiner letters or supervisory findings; job postings for AI model validation versus AI data lineage/logging; and internal architecture diagrams showing whether banks build central evidence stores or extend existing model-risk systems.
