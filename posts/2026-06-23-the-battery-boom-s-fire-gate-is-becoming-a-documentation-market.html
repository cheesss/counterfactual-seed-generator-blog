The Battery Boom's Fire Gate Is Becoming a Documentation Market

The usual battery-storage story starts with demand. Solar-heavy grids need evening capacity, data centers want firming, utilities want fast response, and developers have a growing catalog of containerized systems to buy. On that reading, the bottlenecks are familiar: cells, tariffs, interconnection, transformers, and project finance.

Those constraints are real. DOE put average T&D equipment lead times at 38 weeks in 2023 with nearly 30% year-over-year cost increases, and transformer and switchgear lead times now run from roughly 40 weeks to over 200 (NERC, Wood Mackenzie / ACP). Fire acceptance is not replacing that grid-equipment bottleneck. It is joining it.

The extra pressure is visible in EIA's 2026 storage outlook. EIA says developers and plant owners plan 86 GW of U.S. utility-scale capacity additions in 2026, with battery storage making up 28% of the total. That is 24 GW of planned battery additions, compared with a record 15 GW added in 2025. EIA also lists large scheduled projects such as Lunis Creek at 621 MW, Clear Fork Creek at 600 MW, Bellefield 2 at 500 MW, and Tehuacana Creek 1 at 418 MW. The important word is planned. EIA frames the 2026 total as a record only if realized.

That caveat matters because batteries are no longer just equipment orders. A large BESS has to become three things at once: an interconnectable grid asset, an insurable industrial facility, and a locally acceptable fire risk. The first depends on equipment and network studies. The second depends on loss history, engineering controls, and lender comfort. The third depends on the fire package that lets an authority having jurisdiction sign off.

Moss Landing is why the fire package now matters. EPA records describe the January 2025 incident as a fire at a 300 MW battery energy storage system with about 100,000 lithium-ion batteries and roughly 55% damaged. A later EPA CERCLA order adds details that travel in hearings: about 1,200 evacuees, water-only firefighting, and a respondent-funded removal action beyond the day of the fire.

The public record does not support a simple claim that every large battery site is unsafe. Vistra's own response page says the cause remains unknown, and WECC's December 2025 report says it is not a root-cause analysis. The narrower point is more useful: one high-profile incident can change the questions every later project must answer.

The California clock

California's CEC opt-in pathway is the cleanest test of whether state-level streamlining can absorb that new scrutiny. The CEC says qualifying storage systems capable of storing at least 200 MWh can seek CEC certification, and that the CEC must make a decision within 270 days after determining an application is complete. The fact sheet also shows retained-agency permit decisions by day 360. That sounds fast, but the clock does not start at first filing. It starts after completeness.

Potentia-Viridi makes the distinction concrete. The Alameda County project is a 400 MW / 3,200 MWh BESS on about 102 acres, with a 500-kV generation tie into PG&E's Tesla Substation. CEC records show the application was received on Aug. 7, 2024, deemed incomplete on Sept. 6, 2024, determined complete on Aug. 29, 2025, and approved on May 26, 2026. From completeness to approval, the process hit 270 calendar days. From initial receipt to approval, it took about 657 days.

Potentia's fire records show what fills that clock. The Fire Master Plan names Alameda County Fire as AHJ and CAL FIRE for technical review, and — with no public water at the remote site — two 30,000-gallon emergency tanks. The draft emergency plan assumes LFP chemistry, an unmanned remotely monitored facility, 75-foot staging, full PPE, annual first-responder familiarization, and 24-48 hours of post-incident fire watch.

The live negotiation cases

Corby is the current example of the gate being negotiated in public. The CEC project page lists Corby as a 300 MW / 1,200 MWh opt-in BESS on about 40.3 acres, under review, with a 1.1-mile 230-kV gen-tie to PG&E's Vaca-Dixon Substation and a new PG&E 230-kV double-bus bay. A Sept. 3, 2025 CEC report of conversation records discussion over fire apparatus access, gas and heat detection, BMS data, water supply, response time, toxic-gas hazards, and local wind patterns.

The water discussion is the tell. The applicant objected to a 1,500 gpm requirement and proposed a 24,000-gallon onsite tank designed around roughly 250 gpm, mainly to protect vegetation and inverters rather than cool the involved BESS container. Dixon Fire cited NFPA 1142's 500 gpm minimum and local Appendix B's 1,500 gpm figure. CEC staff noted it can require additional protections beyond fire code if warranted. That is not a yes/no decision. It is a negotiation over what physical backup, staffing, and response evidence will make the project acceptable.

Compass cuts the other way. The San Juan Capistrano project is a 250 MW / 1,000 MWh opt-in BESS on about 13 acres, using LFP or similar batteries, connected to SDG&E's 138-kV system with no offsite transmission upgrades required for full capacity. But on Dec. 22, 2025, ENGIE / Compass asked the CEC to suspend processing while it evaluates alternative sites, resource adequacy, and market changes to improve economic viability.

That matters because not every battery delay should be blamed on fire officials. Compass has an OCFA letter finding less-than-significant impact on public-service needs and response times, even as application materials commit to a hazardous-materials analysis and emergency response plan per OCFA requirements. The record is mixed: opposition is real and fire documents are required, but the pause is explicitly tied to alternative sites and economics.

The local rulebook

Orange County shows how a moratorium can turn into a rulebook. Ordinance 25-017 took effect Dec. 4, 2025 and added Sec. 7-9-122 to the county zoning code. It requires Planning Commission use-permit review, bars BESS facilities on parcels in very high fire hazard severity zones, imposes a 100-foot buffer from community buildings and residential uses unless justified, requires HMA and emergency response / emergency action plans reviewed by County Building & Safety and OCFA, and requires decommissioning plans plus financial assurance before building permits.

The ordinance is not a permanent categorical ban. It converts the temporary pause into a development standard. That distinction is important for the investment logic. A ban destroys the local market. A repeatable rulebook creates a new checklist market: siting analysis, HMA, emergency plans, OCFA review, decommissioning, financial assurance, correction of safety issues, and public-benefit findings.

The fire package is no longer abstract

The strongest new evidence is not a bigger project — it is the paperwork that stands between a battery container and a buildable one.

Kern County Fire Department's 2025 ESS Application Guide is useful because it reads like a working template for a sophisticated AHJ. It calls for construction and operational permits, scaled construction documents, manufacturer specifications, listings, fire suppression, detection, ventilation, deflagration details, commissioning plans, UL 9540 listing, and UL 9540A large-scale fire testing where required. It also turns HMA and FMEA work into a formal review item covering thermal runaway, BMS failure, fire-protection failure, HVAC failure, exhaust failure, smoke detection, fire detection, gas detection, and suppression failure modes. Kern County is not the United States. But it shows what the documentation market can look like when a fire department has a mature checklist.

Pembroke, Massachusetts shows the same gate in a different form. A Code Red Consultants analysis for a six-unit Tesla Megapack 2 XL project (LFP CATL cells) stacks up Tesla UL 9540A reports, TUV unit testing, Fisher Engineering fire-protection analysis, an HMA, an emergency operation plan, and a commissioning plan for the construction-permit stage. Then the local process bit back: Pembroke ZBA minutes from Apr. 13, 2026 show a unanimous remand to the Planning Board for more site-specific fire-protection, emergency-response, and life-safety information.

Project 903 in Armour Township is the clearest small-project warning. The Solarbank / PowerBank proposal is only 4.99 MW / 19.9 MWh, yet it carries a full evidence stack: a Fire & Risk Alliance HMA plus Jensen Hughes risk, explosion, and CFD analyses, with the HMA judging it could meet NFCC / NFPA 855 criteria after design. Armour Township still refused the Official Plan and Zoning amendments on Jan. 13, 2026, citing public safety, emergency-response capacity, firefighter training, and cross-boundary impacts. The lesson is not that every HMA fails; it is that an HMA can be necessary without being sufficient.

Incident records and what they do not prove

The latest incident records sharpen the article's most important caveat: fire cases can reveal the acceptance problem without proving a supplier-wide failure rate.

Victorian Big Battery is the cleanest example. EPRI identifies the Moorabool incident as a 300 MW / 450 MWh Tesla NMC Megapack system, with Tesla as integrator and the project still in construction / commissioning. Energy Safe Victoria found the likely initiating cause was a Megapack cooling-system leak that led to a short circuit, thermal runaway, and spread to a second Megapack. The independent Fisher Engineering / ESRG report adds the more useful permitting lesson: UL 9540A unit-level testing had validated 15 cm spacing without propagation, but the actual incident had 20-30 knot wind, roughly two to three times the UL 9540A unit-test wind condition, and flame impingement exposed a roof / vent propagation path.

That is not a Tesla fleet loss ratio. The record gives project scale, wind-driven propagation, and commissioning-state factors, not a normalized supplier failure rate. The narrower point holds: weather, commissioning state, and enclosure design can matter as much as the laboratory evidence package.

Warwick, New York shows the same at small scale. EPRI logs paired June 2023 events — 8 MW / 36 MWh and 4 MW / 22 MWh, Powin modules at about 0.1-year age — inside Orange & Rockland's 12 MW / 57 MWh non-wires project, and village minutes tie them to water infiltration in specific Powin containers during heavy rain. Leamington shows the softer side: EDF / DNV ran a public process with mailed notices to residents within 300 meters and a community benefit agreement valued at up to $1 million over the project life. None of these yield an insurer loss ratio or a fleet denominator; the proof stays case-level. That is precisely why AHJs and local boards ask for the exact equipment, emergency-response plan, and post-incident modifications rather than a generic safety story.

Who controls the constraint

No single company controls this market. The CEC runs the state approval path; local fire departments and AHJs shape construction documents, water supply, emergency plans, inspection, and operating conditions. Fire-protection specialists (Coffman, Fire & Risk Alliance, Jensen Hughes, Code Red, Fisher Engineering) turn battery hazards into acceptable documents; UL 9540A and NFPA 855 set the evidence language; insurers and lenders decide whether those documents suffice for coverage and financing.

The spend paths are dispersed: testing and certification, fire-protection engineering, HMA and deflagration analysis, monitoring and gas detection, fire-water tanks, access and fencing, training, environmental response, legal work, and insurance. The companies that benefit most are not necessarily the cell suppliers. In a tighter approval environment, the scarce asset is repeatable proof that a project can be built, insured, inspected, and operated without becoming the next incident file.

What would prove this wrong

The strongest counterargument is that standards absorb the shock. If NFPA 855, UL 9540A, LFP chemistry, modular enclosures, better gas detection, insurer templates, and repeated CEC decisions turn fire acceptance into a predictable checklist, the bottleneck becomes manageable. Orange County's ordinance could be evidence for that version if it produces clear approvals rather than long delays.

The second counterargument is that other bottlenecks dominate. Compass is the useful warning: its pause was tied to alternative sites, resource adequacy, and economics, not a fire denial. Transformer and switchgear lead times are already long, tariffs and China-linked supply remain material, and interconnection can still overwhelm siting — EIA's 24 GW plan could miss for reasons that have little to do with fire departments.

What to watch

  • Corby's final fire-protection conditions, especially water supply and response time, and Compass's pause resolution (same site, alternative, or cancellation).
  • Orange County's first BESS applications under Ordinance 25-017, and whether Kern-style AHJ guides spread or stay local exceptions.
  • Pembroke's post-remand outcome and Project 903's OLT process after the Armour refusal.
  • Whether CEC opt-in projects keep hitting the 270-day post-completeness clock, and how wide the first-filing-to-completeness gap runs.
  • Public release or continued confidentiality of UL 9540A large-scale fire-test packages.
  • Insurer requirements for spacing, water supply, monitoring, LEL ventilation, and emergency response.

The battery boom is not just a manufacturing problem anymore. It is a permission, proof, and risk-transfer problem. The visible question is how many containers can be bought. The more revealing question is how many projects can assemble the documents, equipment, water, response plans, and insurance terms that make those containers locally acceptable.
